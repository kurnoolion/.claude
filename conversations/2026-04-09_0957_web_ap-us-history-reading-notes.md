# 📚 APUSH Chapter 24C — The Civil Rights Movement, Election of 1960 & End of the 1950s

## I. THE BEATS & SEEDS OF DISSENT (Brief Context)

- **The Beats** — a literary/cultural movement of the 1950s that *rejected* mainstream American values; specifically opposed:
  - The **work ethic** (the idea that hard work and productivity define a person's worth)
  - **"Desperate materialism"** — their term for the suburban middle class's obsession with consumer goods and status
  - **Militarization** of American life driven by Cold War ideology
- Beats celebrated **impulsive action, immediate pleasure** (often drug-enhanced), and **sexual experimentation**
- Key argument: America preached freedom but actually practiced **personal and political repression**
- **"Howl"** — poem by Beat writer Allen Ginsberg; taken to court on obscenity charges but ruled to have **"redeeming social value"** — an early landmark for free expression
- The Beats were a *precursor* — young white rebels wouldn't find their political cause until the **1960s**

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The Beats are often tested as a *cultural* rather than political movement — know that they criticized Cold War conformity and materialism, not just government policy.

## II. ORIGINS OF THE CIVIL RIGHTS MOVEMENT

### Why It Happened When It Did — Three Root Causes:
1. **Destabilization of the racial system during WWII** — Black soldiers fought for democracy abroad while facing discrimination at home; wartime jobs brought Black workers into new economic roles → exposed contradictions in American society
2. **The Great Migration** — Mass movement of Black Americans out of the segregated South into northern cities made them an **increasingly important voting bloc** within the **Democratic Party coalition** → gave Black Americans political leverage
3. **The Cold War & decolonization** — The rise of **independent nations in the Third World** (newly decolonized African and Asian countries) and the Cold War rivalry with the USSR made American racial segregation an **international embarrassment** — undermined U.S. claims to champion freedom and democracy

### Why the Movement Was Still Surprising:
- **McCarthyism** had decimated (severely weakened/destroyed) traditional left-wing allies of Black Americans
- **Most union leaders** refused to challenge racial inequalities *within their own unions*
- The **NAACP** (National Association for the Advancement of Colored People) was focused mainly on **court battles**, not mass organizing
- Solution: The movement found its organizing base in the **southern Black church** → provided community infrastructure, leadership networks, and moral authority for **nonviolent, militant** resistance

### The Reality of Segregation in the 1950s:
- **Half of Black families lived in poverty**
- **Seniority-based labor contracts** hurt Black workers disproportionately — because they joined the industrial labor force *later* than whites, they were **"last hired, first fired"** during economic downturns
- In the **South**: **Jim Crow laws** mandated separate public facilities — signs reading **"white"** and **"colored"** at entrances, train carriages, drinking fountains, restrooms
  - **Jim Crow** = the system of racial segregation laws enforced primarily in the South after Reconstruction
- In the **North and West**: No *legal* segregation, but **custom** (social practice) barred Black people from many colleges, hotels, restaurants, and nearly all **suburban housing**
  - This is called **de facto segregation** — segregation *in fact* (by practice/custom) rather than by law
  - **De jure segregation** = segregation *by law* (what existed in the South)
- **Las Vegas example**: As segregated as any southern city — Black entertainers like **Lena Horne, Sammy Davis Jr., and Louis Armstrong** performed in hotel-casinos on "the strip" but could not *stay* as guests where they performed
- **17 southern and border states + Washington D.C.** had laws *requiring* racial segregation of public schools
- **~40% of the nation's 28 million schoolchildren** attended legally segregated schools
- Millions more in the North attended **de facto** segregated schools based on housing patterns and school district lines

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** Know the difference between **de facto** (by practice, North) vs. **de jure** (by law, South) segregation — this distinction is frequently tested. Also note that Las Vegas is used as an example showing segregation was *not* just a Southern phenomenon.

## III. THE LEGAL ASSAULT ON SEGREGATION

### LULAC & Mendez v. Westminster (1946):
- **LULAC** — League of United Latin American Citizens; the Latino equivalent of the NAACP; fought restrictive housing, employment discrimination, and school segregation
- **Mendez v. Westminster (1946)** — Federal court ordered the schools of **Orange County, California** desegregated for Latino students → California state legislature repealed all school segregation laws as a result
- **Earl Warren** — Governor of California who signed this desegregation measure; had previously overseen the internment of Japanese-Americans as state attorney general; after WWII, became convinced racial inequality had no place in American life → later appointed **Chief Justice of the Supreme Court** by Eisenhower in **1953**

### NAACP Legal Strategy:
- Led by attorneys **Charles Hamilton Houston** and **Thurgood Marshall**
- Goal: dismantle **Plessy v. Ferguson (1896)** — the Supreme Court ruling that established the **"separate but equal"** doctrine, which justified segregation as long as facilities were nominally equal
- **Strategy Phase 1**: Attack cases where *no* Black equivalent institution existed
  - **1938**: Supreme Court ordered **University of Missouri Law School** to admit **Lloyd Gaines** (Black student) because Missouri had no Black law school → Missouri responded by *building* a segregated law school, technically satisfying the court
  - **1950**: Supreme Court ordered **Heman Sweatt** admitted to **University of Texas Law School** — Texas had created a "school" for him in a *basement* with 3 classrooms and no library; Court said this could never be "equal" to the prestigious white institution

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The Sweatt and Gaines cases are stepping stones to Brown — they show the NAACP chipping away at "separate but equal" *before* attacking it directly.

## IV. BROWN V. BOARD OF EDUCATION

### Background of the Cases:
- **Clarendon County, SC**: **Levi Pearson**, a Black farmer, sued on behalf of his children over *unequal funding* (not segregation itself) — school board spent **$179 per white child** vs. **$43 per black child**; Black schools had no running water, indoor toilets, or school buses; Pearson's house was **burned to the ground** in retaliation
- Five cases from **4 states + Washington D.C.** were combined into a single Supreme Court appeal (1952)

### Why It's Called *Brown*:
- Cases listed **alphabetically** — first case was **Harry Briggs** of South Carolina
- Chief Justice **Earl Warren** didn't want to appear to single out the old Confederacy states → reorganized under a **Midwestern case**
- **Oliver Brown** sued because his daughter (a 3rd grader) was forced to walk across **dangerous railroad tracks** to reach a Black school rather than attend a nearby whites-only school
- Named: **Brown v. Board of Education of Topeka, Kansas**

### Thurgood Marshall's Argument:
- Shifted strategy from attacking *unequal application* of "separate but equal" to attacking **the doctrine itself**
- Argued: Even with identical funding/facilities, segregation is **inherently unequal** because it stigmatizes Black citizens as unfit to associate with others
- Drew on research by **Kenneth and Mamie Clark** (New York psychologists) — their **doll studies** showed segregation caused lasting psychological damage to Black children by undermining **self-esteem**
- **Eisenhower administration's brief**: Did *not* directly support Marshall's argument, but urged the Court to consider the problem in the context of the **Cold War struggle between freedom and tyranny** — other nations couldn't understand how this practice existed in a country claiming to champion democracy

### The Decision — May 17, 1954:
- Chief Justice Warren achieved **unanimity** on a divided Court (some justices feared widespread violence but Warren persuaded them all)
- Decision: Only **11 pages long**
- Ruling: Segregation in public education violated the **Equal Protection Clause of the 14th Amendment**
- Key quote: **"In the field of education, the doctrine of 'separate but equal' has no place. Separate educational facilities are inherently unequal."**
- Black press called it a **"second Emancipation Proclamation"**

### Limitations of Brown:
- Did **not** address segregation in institutions *other than* public schools
- Did **not** ban all racial classifications (e.g., laws against interracial marriage untouched)
- Did **not** address **de facto** school segregation in the North
- Did **not** order immediate implementation → called for hearings on *how* to dismantle segregation
- **1955 implementation ruling**: Ordered desegregation proceed **"with all deliberate speed"** — vague language that *unintentionally* enabled resistance

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** "With all deliberate speed" is a trap answer — it sounds decisive but was actually *vague* and enabled delay. Also know Brown applied *only* to public schools, not all segregation.

## V. MASSIVE RESISTANCE

- **Southern Manifesto (1956)**: Signed by **96 of 106 southern congressmen** and **every southern senator** except **Lyndon B. Johnson** (TX), **Albert Gore** (TN), and **Estes Kefauver** (TN) → denounced Brown as a **"clear abuse of judicial power"**, called for resistance by **"any lawful means"**
- State-level resistance tactics:
  - Made it **illegal for the NAACP** to operate within their borders
  - **Virginia's strategy**: Close any public school ordered to desegregate; offer state funds to white (but not Black) pupils to attend *private* institutions
  - **Prince Edward County, VA**: Shut its public schools entirely in **1959**; Supreme Court didn't order them reopened until **1964**
  - **"Freedom of choice" plans**: Allowed white students to opt out of integrated schools
- **Confederate symbolism as defiance**: Georgia incorporated the **Confederate battle flag** into its state flag in **1956**; Alabama and South Carolina flew the battle flag over their state capitol buildings

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The Southern Manifesto is often tested — know the number (96 of 106), the exceptions (LBJ, Gore, Kefauver), and that it called for resistance by *lawful* means specifically.

## VI. EISENHOWER & CIVIL RIGHTS

- Eisenhower **failed to provide moral leadership** on civil rights — called for obeying the law but made clear he found the whole issue *distasteful*; privately disagreed with the Supreme Court's reasoning in Brown
- **Autherine Lucy (1956)**: Federal court ordered her admitted to **University of Alabama** → a mob prevented her from registering; board of trustees **expelled her**; Eisenhower did nothing; university stayed all-white into the 1960s
- **Civil Rights Act of 1957**: First civil rights law since Reconstruction; pushed through by Senate Majority Leader **Lyndon B. Johnson** (who wanted liberal support for a 1960 presidential run); targeted denial of **Black voting rights** in the South; had **weak enforcement provisions** → added few voters to the rolls
- **Little Rock Crisis (1957)**: Governor **Orval Faubus** of Arkansas used the **National Guard** to block court-ordered integration of **Little Rock's Central High School** → Eisenhower sent **federal troops** (soldiers of the **101st Airborne Division**) to escort **nine Black students** (the "Little Rock Nine") into the school in the face of a mob
  - Showed: The federal government *would* ultimately enforce court orders when flagrantly violated
  - Result: By the time Eisenhower left office, **fewer than 2%** of Black students in the old Confederacy states attended desegregated schools

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** Little Rock is significant because Eisenhower acted *not* out of moral conviction but because a *state* was defying *federal* court orders — a federalism issue, not a civil rights commitment.

## VII. THE MONTGOMERY BUS BOYCOTT

### Rosa Parks:
- **December 1, 1955**: Rosa Parks, a Black tailor's assistant in **Montgomery, Alabama**, refused to give up her bus seat to a white rider as required by local law → arrested
- Often misremembered as simply a tired seamstress — in reality, a **veteran civil rights activist**:
  - Participated in protests over the **Scottsboro Boys** (1930s) — nine Black teenagers falsely accused of rape in Alabama
  - Served as secretary to **E.D. Nixon**, local NAACP leader
  - Tried to register to vote in **1943** — turned away by a phony literacy test; succeeded on her **third attempt**, becoming one of very few Black Montgomery residents able to vote
  - Attended **1954 training session** at the **Highlander School** in Tennessee — a meeting ground for labor and civil rights radicals

### Why Parks Was Chosen:
- An earlier challenge to bus segregation was abandoned because the person involved was an **unmarried pregnant teenager** — not considered an ideal public symbol
- Parks was **dignified and accomplished** — ideal as a movement symbol
- Timing may have been chosen because an all-white Mississippi jury had just **acquitted the murderers of Emmett Till** — a Black teenager allegedly killed for whistling at a white woman; his parents insisted on an **open casket** so the nation could see his severely disfigured body

### The Boycott:
- **Jo Ann Robinson** — professor at all-Black Alabama State University; had been calling for a bus boycott since **1954**; played a leading organizational role: negotiated with city officials, edited the boycott newsletter, drove people to/from work
- **381 days** of boycott — Black maids, janitors, teachers, and students walked or used an informal taxi network despite **legal harassment and occasional violence**
- **November 1956**: Supreme Court ruled **segregation in public transportation unconstitutional** → boycott ended in victory

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** Jo Ann Robinson is a frequently overlooked figure — know her role as an organizer, not just Parks. Also know the *exact* length: 381 days.

## VIII. THE EMERGENCE OF MARTIN LUTHER KING JR.

- **Age 26** when the boycott began; had recently arrived in Montgomery as pastor of a Baptist church
- His **philosophy of nonviolent resistance** drew from:
  - **Henry David Thoreau** — American writer; concept of **peaceful civil disobedience** (refusing to obey unjust laws)
  - **Mohandas (Mahatma) Gandhi** — Indian independence leader; proved nonviolent mass protest could defeat a powerful oppressor
  - **CORE** (Congress of Racial Equality) — organized nonviolent protests in the **1940s**
- King framed the struggle using the language of **freedom**, appealing to both Black communities and white America by connecting Black rights to core American values
- Like **Frederick Douglass**, King stressed protesters' **love of country and devotion to national values**
- Like **W.E.B. Du Bois**, King connected American racism to **global colonialism** — linked the "color line" with the oppression of non-white peoples worldwide: *"If Africa was gaining its freedom, why must Black America lag behind?"*
- First book: ***Stride Toward Freedom*** — about the Montgomery boycott
- **"I Have a Dream" speech (1963)**: Invoked the unfulfilled promise of emancipation; ended with lines borrowed from a Black spiritual: *"Free at last! Free at last! Thank God Almighty, we are free at last!"*

### Southern Christian Leadership Conference (SCLC):
- Founded **1956** by King and other Black ministers and civil rights activists
- Goal: Press for desegregation using the **Black church** as organizational base

### The Meaning of "Freedom" in the Movement:
- **Specific freedoms**: Access to public libraries, lunch counters, department stores (central sites of consumer culture); being addressed as "Mr./Miss/Mrs." instead of "boy" and "auntie"
- **Legal freedom**: Equal political rights, economic opportunity, end of disenfranchisement, end of confinement to low-wage jobs
- **Freedom of the mind**: Liberation from years of deference to and fear of whites
- **Freedom Schools (Summer 1964)**: Civil rights activists established schools for Black children across Mississippi — began by asking students to *define* the word "freedom"

## IX. CIVIL RIGHTS & THE COLD WAR / INTERNATIONAL CONTEXT

- American leaders feared **international damage** from segregation since the start of the Cold War
- **Truman** promoted civil rights partly to show the world the U.S. was serious about freedom
- **State Department** filed a brief in the *Brown* case noting how segregation was damaging America's **global image**
- Global reaction to *Brown* was **overwhelmingly positive** — example: Senegalese newspaper headline: *"At Last! Whites and Blacks in the United States on the same school benches!"*
- Slow pace of change → **embarrassed American diplomats** trying to win loyalty in the non-white world; in India, the American ambassador was questioned: Was the Haitian ambassador forced to live in a Black ghetto in Washington? Why did no Black person hold high public office?
- **Soviet Union** exploited American race relations as propaganda in the Cold War **"battle for hearts and minds"**

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The Cold War context for civil rights is a major AP theme — U.S. racial hypocrisy damaged America's credibility with newly independent Third World nations. This is why even reluctant politicians like Eisenhower eventually had to act.

## X. THE ELECTION OF 1960

### Candidates:
- **Republican**: **Richard Nixon** — incumbent Vice President under Eisenhower
- **Democrat**: **John F. Kennedy (JFK)** — Senator from Massachusetts; Roman Catholic; 43 years old; **youngest major-party presidential nominee** in U.S. history; father was a millionaire Irish-American businessman and former ambassador to Britain
- Kennedy's rivals for the Democratic nomination: **Hubert Humphrey** (liberal wing leader) and **Lyndon B. Johnson** (TX, Senate Majority Leader) → Johnson accepted Kennedy's offer to be **VP nominee**

### The Catholic Question:
- As recently as **1949**, Paul Blanshard's *American Freedom and Catholic Power* was a national bestseller — accused the Catholic Church of being antidemocratic, morally repressive, and un-American
- Many Protestants feared Kennedy would take orders from the **Pope** or be bound by Church doctrine on public issues
- Kennedy's response: *"I do not speak for my church on public matters, and the church does not speak for me"*
- His victory in the **West Virginia Democratic primary** (an overwhelmingly *Protestant* state) effectively settled the religion question

### Key Issues — The Cold War:
- Both Kennedy and Nixon were **ardent Cold Warriors**
- Kennedy attacked Republicans for allowing a **"missile gap"** to develop — claimed the Soviets had achieved **technological and military superiority** after:
  - **Sputnik** — first Earth satellite, launched by the USSR
  - **Intercontinental Ballistic Missile (ICBM)** — first tested by the Soviets
- **Reality**: Both Kennedy and Nixon privately *knew* the missile gap was a **myth** — American military and economic capacity far exceeded Soviet capacity — but the charge was politically effective

### The First Televised Presidential Debate:
- Kennedy appeared **handsome, calm, and confident** on TV; Nixon was suffering from a cold, appeared **tired and pale**
- **TV viewers** thought Kennedy won; **radio listeners** thought Nixon won
- Demonstrated that in the **television age, image matters as much as (or more than) substance**
- Jackie Kennedy's stylishness contrasted with Pat Nixon's and Mamie Eisenhower's more conservative appearances — reinforced the image of Kennedy as youthful and vigorous

### Result:
- Kennedy won by **~120,000 votes** out of **69 million cast** — one of the **closest elections in American history**
- Republicans accused Kennedy of benefiting from **fraudulent vote counts** by the notoriously corrupt **Chicago Democratic machine**

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The missile gap was a *myth* that both candidates knew about — Kennedy used it anyway for political advantage. The televised debate is a landmark in the role of media in politics.

## XI. EISENHOWER'S FAREWELL ADDRESS & THE END OF THE 1950s

### The Farewell Address (January 1961):
- Modeled on **George Washington's Farewell Address of 1796**
- Knowing the missile gap was a myth, Eisenhower warned against calls for a new military buildup
- Coined the term **"military-industrial complex"**: the dangerous alliance between:
  - **"An immense military establishment"** (the Pentagon/Defense Department)
  - **"A permanent arms industry"** (private defense contractors)
- Warned this combination's influence was felt in **"every office"** in the land and could **"endanger our liberties or democratic processes"**
- Most Americans **dismissed his concern** — saw the defense-industry alliance as a source of **jobs and national security**, not a threat
- Vindicated later when the U.S. became locked in an **increasingly unpopular war** (Vietnam)

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** "Military-industrial complex" is one of the most tested terms from this era — Eisenhower, a *former general*, warned against it, which is the irony. Know it came from his *Farewell Address* (1961).

### Environmental & Social Cracks in 1950s Life:
- **Leaded gasoline**: Cars spewing toxic **lead** (added to gasoline to improve efficiency) into the atmosphere — **Los Angeles** became synonymous with **smog** (air pollution produced by automobile emissions trapped by surrounding mountains)
- **Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs)**: Used in air conditioners, deodorants, and aerosol hair sprays → released chemicals that **trapped heat** (contributing to **global warming**) and **damaged the ozone layer** (increasing skin cancer rates)
- **Thomas Midgley** — General Motors research scientist who *invented both* leaded gasoline AND chlorofluorocarbons; described as having had **"more impact on the atmosphere than any other single organism"** in world history
- **Chemical insecticides**: Enabled agricultural abundance but **poisoned farmworkers, consumers, and the water supply**
- **Suburban housewives**: Increasingly rebelling against life centered in suburban dream houses
- **Black Americans**: Increasingly impatient with the slow pace of racial change

## I. THE RISING TIDE OF PROTEST — SIT-INS & SNCC

### The Sit-In Movement:
- **Sit-ins** — nonviolent protest tactic where Black students sat at segregated lunch counters and refused to leave when denied service; forced the issue of segregation into public view
- College students emerged for the **first time** as the **leading force for social change** in American history
- **April 1960**: **Ella Baker** — a longtime civil rights organizer — called a meeting of young activists in **Raleigh, North Carolina**; ~200 Black students and a few whites attended

### SNCC — Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee:
- **Founded**: April 1960, out of Ella Baker's Raleigh meeting
- **Goal**: Replace the culture of segregation with a **"beloved community"** of racial justice; empower ordinary Black people to take control of decisions affecting their lives
- Key philosophy: Bottom-up organizing — empower regular people, not just leaders
- **Robert Moses** — SNCC organizer; argued adults were too afraid of the pressure they'd face → **"This leaves the young people to be the organizers, the agents of social and political change"**
- SNCC would later become more radical; its evolution is a key arc of this chapter

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** SNCC vs. SCLC — SNCC was **student-led**, more grassroots and confrontational; SCLC was King's **minister-led** organization. Know the difference in approach and leadership.

## II. FREEDOM RIDES (1961)

- Organized by **CORE** (Congress of Racial Equality)
- **Purpose**: Test compliance with court orders banning segregation on **interstate buses and trains** and in **terminal facilities**
- **Method**: Integrated groups (Black and white together) traveled by bus into the **Lower South**
- **Violence**:
  - Near **Anniston, Alabama**: A **firebomb** was thrown into a bus; passengers beaten as they escaped
  - In **Birmingham**: Klansmen attacked riders with **bats and chains** while **police refused to intervene**
  - Many Freedom Riders were **arrested**
- **Result**: Their actions forced the **Interstate Commerce Commission (ICC)** to order buses and terminals **desegregated**

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The Freedom Rides tested *existing* court orders — they weren't just protesting in general; they were enforcing what courts had *already* ruled. The ICC order was the tangible legal victory.

## III. ALBANY, GEORGIA & JAMES MEREDITH

### Albany Movement (Late 1961–1962):
- SNCC and other groups launched a campaign of **nonviolent protests** against racial discrimination in **Albany, Georgia**
- Lasted a **full year**
- Key tactic: **"Filling the jails"** — mass arrests designed to overwhelm the system and gain **national sympathy**
- **Result: Failed** — did not achieve its goals; Albany showed that nonviolent protest alone wasn't always sufficient without federal support or media attention that forced action

### James Meredith & University of Mississippi (1962):
- **September 1962**: A court ordered the **University of Mississippi** to admit **James Meredith**, a Black student
- Governor **Ross Barnett** of Mississippi encouraged resistance → a **mob** rampaged through **Oxford, Mississippi** (location of the university)
- **Two bystanders killed** in the riot
- President Kennedy was **forced to dispatch the army** to restore order

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** Albany *failed* — this is important because it shows nonviolent protest wasn't automatically successful. The lesson shaped how King approached **Birmingham** (next section) — he needed media-friendly confrontation, not just arrests.

---

## IV. BIRMINGHAM, ALABAMA (Spring 1963)

### Background:
- Spring 1963: Demonstrations across the South in **186 cities** — **15,000+ arrests in a single week** in June
- **Birmingham** was the dramatic focal point — described as a **"citadel of segregation"**
- Even by Lower South standards, Birmingham was extraordinarily violent: **50+ bombings** of Black homes and institutions since WWII
- Local Black community had been demonstrating for **greater economic opportunity** and an end to segregation by local businesses — with **no result**

### King's "Letter from Birmingham Jail" (April 1963):
- King was serving a **9-day prison term** for violating a ban on demonstrations
- Wrote one of history's most eloquent defenses of civil disobedience — addressed to **local clergymen** who counseled *patience*
- Key arguments:
  - Described the daily **litany of abuses** Black southerners faced — police brutality, humiliation of explaining to children why they can't enter amusement parks or public pools
  - Criticized the **"white moderate"** — someone who preferred *order* to *justice* and asked Black people to wait; King said this was more of an obstacle than outright racists
  - Justified **nonviolent direct action** as the appropriate response to unjust laws

### The Children's Crusade:
- King made the **bold decision** to send **Black schoolchildren** into the streets of Birmingham
- Police chief **Eugene "Bull" Connor** unleashed police against the young marchers with:
  - **Nightsticks**
  - **High-pressure fire hoses**
  - **Attack dogs**
- Images broadcast on **television** worldwide produced a wave of **revulsion** and turned Birmingham into a **global symbol of American racial brutality**
- **Result**: Leading businessmen — fearing international embarrassment — brokered a deal:
  - **Desegregated** downtown stores and restaurants
  - Promised **Black salespeople** would be hired
- More importantly: Forced **President Kennedy** to publicly endorse the civil rights movement's goals

### Violence Escalates After Birmingham:
- **June 1963**: Sniper killed **Medgar Evers** — field secretary of the NAACP in Mississippi — outside his home
- **September 1963**: A bomb exploded at a **Black Baptist church in Birmingham**, killing **four young girls**
  - Last perpetrator not tried and convicted until **2002** (nearly 40 years later)

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** "Bull" Connor is frequently tested — his brutal response *helped* the movement by generating outrage. This is the paradox of Birmingham: the overreaction of segregationists advanced civil rights. Also know the "Letter from Birmingham Jail" was addressed to *white moderates/clergymen*, not to the government.

---

## V. THE MARCH ON WASHINGTON (August 28, 1963)

- **Date**: August 28, 1963 — **two weeks before** the Birmingham church bombing
- **250,000** Black and white Americans converged on Washington D.C.
- Organized by a coalition of civil rights, labor, and church organizations led by **A. Philip Randolph** — the Black labor leader who had threatened a similar march in **1941** to pressure FDR
- **Largest public demonstration in U.S. history** up to that point
- **Slogan**: **"Jobs and Freedom"** — revealed the movement had forged an alliance with **white liberal groups**

### Goals (broader than just civil rights):
- Passage of the **civil rights bill** pending in Congress
- A **public-works program** to reduce unemployment
- An increase in the **minimum wage**
- A law **barring discrimination in employment**

### King's "I Have a Dream" Speech:
- Delivered on the steps of the **Lincoln Memorial**
- Invoked the Declaration of Independence: *"I have a dream that one day this nation will rise up and live out the true meaning of its creed: 'We hold these truths to be self-evident, that all men are created equal'"*
- Closed with lines from a Black spiritual: *"Free at last! Free at last! Thank God Almighty, we are free at last!"*

### Tensions Within the March:
- **Gender exclusion**: Despite women like **Jo Ann Robinson** and **Ella Baker** playing crucial organizing roles, **every speaker at the Lincoln Memorial was male**
- **SNCC's John Lewis** (later a congressman from Georgia) was forced by march organizers to **tone down his speech** — his original text called on Blacks to:
  - *"Free ourselves of the chains of political and economic slavery"*
  - March *"through the heart of Dixie the way Sherman did... and burn Jim Crow to the ground"*
  - Lewis's censored rhetoric **foreshadowed** the more **militant turn** the movement would soon take

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The March on Washington's goals included **economic demands** (jobs, wages), not just civil rights — this is often tested. Also know A. Philip Randolph organized it — the same man who threatened the 1941 march that led to FDR's Executive Order 8802 banning discrimination in defense industries.

---

## VI. THE KENNEDY YEARS

### Kennedy's Character & Priorities:
- Served **less than 3 years**; few tangible domestic accomplishments
- **Inaugural Address (January 1961)**: *"The torch has been passed to a new generation of Americans"*; *"Ask not what your country can do for you; ask what you can do for your country"*
- Despite the sit-ins being a year old at his inauguration, the speech said **nothing about segregation or race**
- At the outset: Regarded civil rights as a **distraction** from his primary concern — **vigorous conduct of the Cold War**

### Kennedy's Foreign Policy Initiatives:
- **Peace Corps**: Sent young Americans abroad to aid the **economic and educational progress** of developing countries; improve U.S. image in the Third World; by **1966**, more than **15,000** volunteers were serving
- **Space program**: When the Soviets launched a satellite carrying the **first man into Earth orbit** (April 1961), Kennedy announced the U.S. would land a man on the **moon by the end of the decade** — accomplished in **1969**
- **Alliance for Progress**: A foreign policy toward Latin America — described as a **"Marshall Plan for the Western Hemisphere"** (though involving far less money); aimed to promote **"political and material freedom"** and counter communism's appeal
  - **Result: Failed** — unlike the Marshall Plan, **military regimes and local elites** controlled the aid; they enriched themselves while the poor saw little benefit

### Cuba & The Bay of Pigs (April 1961):
- **Fidel Castro** led a 1959 revolution that overthrew Cuban dictator **Fulgencio Batista**
- Before Castro: Cuba was an **economic dependency of the United States**
- Castro's government **nationalized American landholdings**, signed a sugar deal with the **Soviet Union** → Eisenhower suspended trade/diplomatic relations and had the CIA begin training **anti-Castro exiles** for an invasion
- **Bay of Pigs invasion (April 1961)**: Kennedy allowed the CIA to launch the exile invasion
  - Military advisers predicted a **popular uprising** that would topple Castro — this never happened
  - **Total failure**: Of 1,400 invaders, **100+ were killed** and **1,100 captured**
  - Result: Cuba became **more closely tied to the Soviet Union**
  - Kennedy administration continued trying to eliminate Castro — including **assassination attempts**

### The Berlin Wall (August 1961):
- Soviets constructed a **wall separating East and West Berlin** to stop the flow of **emigrants fleeing from East to West**
- Stood until **1989** — became the defining **symbol of the Cold War** and the division of Europe

### The Cuban Missile Crisis (October 1962):
- **American spy planes** discovered the Soviet Union was installing **nuclear missiles in Cuba** capable of reaching the United States
- Kennedy **rejected military leaders' advice** to attack Cuba (which would almost certainly have triggered Soviet retaliation in Berlin and possibly nuclear war)
- Instead, Kennedy imposed a **naval blockade ("quarantine")** of Cuba and demanded missiles be removed
- **13 days** on the brink of all-out nuclear war
- **Resolution**:
  - Soviet Premier **Nikita Khrushchev** agreed to withdraw the missiles
  - Kennedy pledged the U.S. **would not invade Cuba**
  - Kennedy **secretly** agreed to remove **American Jupiter missiles from Turkey** (from which they could reach the Soviet Union)
- **Impact on Kennedy**: Shocked by how casually military leaders spoke of **"winning" a nuclear exchange** in which tens of millions would die
- **1963 Nuclear Test Ban Treaty**: U.S. and USSR agreed to ban testing nuclear weapons **in the atmosphere and in space**; Kennedy praised the small **anti-nuclear weapons movement** that had urged this ban

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The secret Jupiter missiles removal from Turkey is a frequently tested detail — the public deal was just the Soviet missile withdrawal, but the *secret* concession was the U.S. pulling missiles from Turkey. Also know Kennedy chose blockade over military strike.

### Kennedy & Civil Rights:
- **First two years**: Preoccupied with foreign policy; reluctant to take a forceful stand on Black demands
- Shared **J. Edgar Hoover's** (FBI director) fear that the movement was **inspired by communism**
- **Attorney General Robert F. Kennedy** (the president's brother) approved **FBI wiretaps on King**
- Waited until **end of 1962** to ban discrimination in federally assisted housing (had promised it during the 1960 campaign)
- Used federal force only when obstruction became acute (e.g., University of Mississippi) — insisted **"law enforcement is a local matter"** rather than protecting civil rights workers
- **After Birmingham (May 1963)**: Kennedy's hand was forced — realized the U.S. couldn't proclaim itself champion of freedom worldwide while maintaining racial inequality at home
- **June 1963**: Went on national television calling for a law banning **discrimination in all places of public accommodation**
- Framed it as a **moral crisis**: *"We preach freedom around the world... but are we to say... that this is a land of the free except for Negroes?"*

### Kennedy's Assassination (November 22, 1963):
- Shot and killed while riding in a motorcade through **Dallas, Texas**
- Most likely assassin: **Lee Harvey Oswald** — a "troubled former marine"
- Oswald was **murdered two days later** by **Jack Ruby**, a local nightclub owner, while in police custody → fueled **conspiracy theories** that persist today
- Kennedy did **not live to see his civil rights bill enacted**
- Compared in national impact to **Pearl Harbor** and **September 11** — an entire generation remembered exactly where they were when they heard

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** Kennedy's civil rights bill was passed by *LBJ*, not Kennedy. Kennedy was more a Cold War president forced into civil rights; LBJ was the one who made it law. This distinction matters for MCQs.

## VII. LYNDON JOHNSON'S PRESIDENCY

### LBJ's Background vs. Kennedy:
- Kennedy: raised in a **wealthy, powerful family**; viewed success as a birthright
- Johnson: grew up in **central Texas hill country** — one of the **poorest parts of the United States**; had to struggle for wealth and power
- Rose to become **Senate Majority Leader** by the 1950s
- Never forgot the **poor Mexican and white children** he taught in a Texas school in the **early 1930s**
- Far more interested than Kennedy in **domestic reform**; held the **New Deal view** that government had an obligation to assist less fortunate members of society

---

## VIII. THE CIVIL RIGHTS ACT OF 1964

- Just **5 days after Kennedy's assassination**, Johnson called on Congress to pass the civil rights bill as the most fitting **memorial to his slain predecessor**
- *"We have talked long enough about equal rights in this country. It is now time to write the next chapter and write it in the books of law."*

### What the Civil Rights Act of 1964 Did:
- **Prohibited racial discrimination** in:
  - Employment
  - Institutions like hospitals and schools
  - **Privately owned public accommodations**: restaurants, hotels, theaters
- **Banned discrimination on the basis of sex** — this provision was added by *opponents* of civil rights hoping it would sink the entire bill; liberal and female members of Congress **embraced it** to broaden the law's scope
- Johnson privately predicted: *"I think we delivered the South to the Republican Party"* — recognizing the massive political cost of passing civil rights legislation

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The sex discrimination ban in the Civil Rights Act of 1964 was originally added by *opponents* as a **poison pill** to kill the bill — this is a classic MCQ trick. Also know this act covered *private* businesses (restaurants, hotels), unlike earlier, more limited legislation.

---

## IX. FREEDOM SUMMER (1964)

- The 1964 Civil Rights Act **did not address voting rights** in the South
- **Summer 1964**: Coalition of SNCC, CORE, and NAACP launched a **voter registration drive in Mississippi**
- Hundreds of **white college students from the North** traveled to Mississippi to participate
- **Violence**: 35 bombings, numerous beatings of civil rights workers

### The Murders of Schwerner, Chaney & Goodman:
- **June 1964**: Three activists kidnapped and murdered near **Philadelphia, Mississippi** by a group headed by a **deputy sheriff**:
  - **Michael Schwerner** — white student from the North
  - **Andrew Goodman** — white student from the North
  - **James Chaney** — local Black youth from Mississippi
- Between 1961–1965: An estimated **25 Black civil rights workers** were killed
- The deaths of the **two white students** focused **unprecedented national attention** on Mississippi — revealing the apparent inability of the federal government to protect citizens exercising constitutional rights
- Not until **June 2005** — **41 years later** — was a KKK member convicted of manslaughter in their deaths

### Mississippi Freedom Democratic Party (MFDP):
- Because Black Mississippians couldn't participate in the regular Democratic Party or even register to vote, the civil rights movement created the **MFDP** — open to **all residents** of the state
- At the **1964 Democratic National Convention in Atlantic City, NJ**, the MFDP challenged the **all-white official Mississippi delegation** for their seats
- **Fannie Lou Hamer** — MFDP leader; gave riveting televised testimony before the credentials committee about growing up in poverty in the **Yazoo-Mississippi Delta** and the **savage beatings** she endured at the hands of police
  - *"Is this America, the land of the free and home of the brave, where we are threatened daily because we want to live as decent human beings?"*
  - Deeply religious; believed Christianity rested on the idea of freedom; saw the movement as **divinely inspired**
- **LBJ's concern**: Feared a **southern walkout** at the convention (as had happened at the **1948 convention**)
- **Compromise offered**: Two Black delegates would be granted seats (as at-large, non-voting delegates); **MFDP rejected this**
- **Impact**: Severely weakened Black activists' faith in the **responsiveness of the political system**; foreshadowed the **breakup of the coalition** between the civil rights movement and the liberal wing of the Democratic Party

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** Fannie Lou Hamer and the MFDP at Atlantic City is a key moment of disillusionment — the Democratic Party chose political calculation (not alienating white Southern Democrats) over justice. This moment helped radicalize SNCC.

---

## X. THE ELECTION OF 1964

### Barry Goldwater:
- **Senator from Arizona**; Republican nominee
- Published ***The Conscience of a Conservative* (1960)** — sold **3 million+ copies**; became the intellectual bible of the new conservative movement
- Key positions:
  - More **aggressive Cold War** conduct (suggested nuclear war might be *"the price of freedom"*)
  - Against the **New Deal welfare state** — believed it stifled individual initiative
  - Wanted to substitute **private charity** for public welfare and Social Security
  - Called for abolition of the **graduated income tax**
  - **Voted against the Civil Rights Act of 1964**
- Acceptance speech: *"Extremism in the defense of liberty is no vice"* — became an explosive, widely-quoted statement

### The Election Result:
- Democrats branded Goldwater an **extremist** who would repeal Social Security and risk nuclear war
- **Johnson**: ~**43 million votes**; **Goldwater**: ~**27 million votes** — **landslide defeat** for Goldwater
- Democrats won **two-to-one majorities** in both houses of Congress

### Why 1964 Mattered for Conservatism (Despite Goldwater's Loss):
- **Goldwater carried 5 states of the Lower South** — showing that opposing civil rights could win Southern votes for Republicans
- **George Wallace** (segregationist governor of Alabama) showed surprisingly strong support in **Democratic primaries in Wisconsin, Indiana, and Maryland** — proving white opposition to civil rights extended *beyond* the South
- **Proposition 14 in California**: A popular referendum **repealed a 1963 fair housing law** banning racial discrimination in real estate sales; backed by realtors using the language of **homeowners' "freedom"** to control their property; passed with **three-fourths of white votes** even though Johnson carried California overall
- **Young Americans for Freedom (YAF)**: Founded **1960**; college-aged conservative activists who became Goldwater's ground troops
  - **Sharon Statement**: YAF's founding document (named for William F. Buckley's estate in **Sharon, Connecticut**) — argued the **free market** underpinned personal freedom, government must be strictly limited, and **international communism** must be destroyed
  - Paralleled the liberal **Port Huron Statement** (SDS, 1962) in claiming youth as the cutting edge of a new movement
- **Sunbelt funding base**: **Orange County, California** (defense industry workers) and **oilmen and aerospace entrepreneurs** from the Southwest provided a new financial base for conservatism
- **National Review** — conservative magazine that had defended Black disenfranchisement and called whites *"the advanced race"* in the 1950s; by the 1960s, conservatives shifted language to **"law and order," "freedom of association,"** and opposition to **welfare** — coded racial appeals

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** 1964 is a *turning point for conservatism*, not just a Democratic landslide. Goldwater's Southern success and the "southern strategy" it opened up is one of the most important political shifts in modern American history. Know the **Sharon Statement** as the conservative counterpart to the **Port Huron Statement**.

## XI. THE VOTING RIGHTS ACT OF 1965

### Selma Campaign:
- **January 1965**: King launched a voting rights campaign in **Selma, Alabama** — where only **355 of 15,000** Black residents had been allowed to register to vote
- **March 1965**: King defied a ban by Governor **George Wallace** and attempted to lead a march from Selma to the state capital, **Montgomery**
- **"Bloody Sunday"**: When marchers reached the bridge leaving the city, **state police assaulted them** with **cattle prods, whips, and tear gas**
- Once again, **television images** of nonviolent demonstrators being brutalized compelled federal action

### LBJ's Response:
- Called Selma a milestone in *"man's unending search for freedom"*
- Asked Congress to enact a voting rights law
- Closed his speech by quoting the movement's song: **"We Shall Overcome"** — the most powerful endorsement of the civil rights movement ever given by a sitting president

### The Voting Rights Act of 1965:
- Allowed **federal officials to register voters** directly — bypassing discriminatory state and local officials
- **Result**: Black southerners finally regained the suffrage that had been stripped from them **at the turn of the twentieth century**
- **24th Amendment**: (companion measure) **outlawed the poll tax** — which had prevented poor Blacks (and some whites) from voting in the South

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** Know the difference: **Civil Rights Act of 1964** = public accommodations and employment; **Voting Rights Act of 1965** = the right to vote. The 24th Amendment (poll tax) is separate from the Voting Rights Act but related in purpose.

---

## XII. THE HART-CELLER ACT / IMMIGRATION REFORM (1965)

- The success of the civil rights movement — establishing that **racism should no longer be a foundation of public policy** — spilled over into immigration law
- **Hart-Celler Act (Immigration Act of 1965)**:
  - **Abolished** the **national-origins quota system** (in place since **1924**) which had excluded Asians and severely restricted southern and eastern Europeans
  - Established **racially neutral criteria**: **family reunification** and possession of **skills in demand** in the U.S.
  - Established the **first limit on Western Hemisphere immigration**: **120,000** (previously there had been no cap)
  - Set a **maximum annual quota of 20,000 persons per country** worldwide
  - Created the category of **"illegal aliens"** from the Americas for the first time
  - Because labor demand for Mexican immigrants far exceeded 20,000/year, the law **guaranteed** a large portion of Mexican immigration would be **unauthorized**
  - Special provisions for **refugees from communist countries** allowed immigration to exceed the caps
- **Unexpected results**:
  - At the time, immigrants were only **5% of the American population** — the lowest since the **1830s**
  - No one anticipated an **explosive rise in immigration** and a dramatic shift toward newcomers from **Latin America, the Caribbean, and Asia** rather than Europe
  - By **1976**: **85%** of poll respondents agreed the U.S. was *"a country made up of many races, religions, and nationalities"* — triumph of a **pluralist conception of Americanism**

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The Hart-Celler Act's unintended consequences are frequently tested — it was *not* expected to dramatically shift immigrant demographics toward Latin America and Asia, but it did. Also know it created the modern concept of "illegal alien" by capping Western Hemisphere immigration for the first time.

## XIII. THE GREAT SOCIETY

### Overview:
- After his 1964 landslide, LBJ outlined the most sweeping proposal for government action since the **New Deal**
- **Great Society** = Johnson's domestic reform agenda, primarily enacted **1965–1967**
- Key difference from the New Deal: **Response to prosperity**, not depression; funded by rapid economic growth and a **1964 tax cut** on individuals and businesses (initially proposed by Kennedy)

### Major Programs:
| Program | What It Did |
|---|---|
| **Medicare** | Health services for the **elderly** |
| **Medicaid** | Health services for the **poor** |
| **Federal Education Funding** | Poured federal money into education and urban development |
| **Dept. of Transportation** | New cabinet office created |
| **Dept. of Housing and Urban Development (HUD)** | New cabinet office for housing policy |
| **Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC)** | Enforced anti-discrimination in employment |
| **National Endowment for the Humanities (NEH)** | Federal arts/humanities funding |
| **National Endowment for the Arts (NEA)** | Federal arts funding |
| **Public Broadcasting** | Created national public broadcasting network (PBS/NPR precursor) |

- These measures **greatly expanded federal government power** and completed the social agenda (minus national health insurance) stalled in Congress since **1938**

### The War on Poverty:
- Centerpiece of the Great Society — launched early **1964**
- Inspired in part by **Michael Harrington's *The Other America* (1962)** — revealed that **40–50 million Americans** lived in poverty, often in "invisible" isolated rural areas or urban slums; crucially pointed out that **whites were the majority of the nation's poor**
- **1960s poverty diagnosis**: Unlike the New Deal (which blamed structural economic flaws), the Johnson administration blamed poverty on **absence of skills** and **lack of proper attitudes and work habits** — this shaped what programs were built
- What the War on Poverty **did NOT do** (notable omissions):
  - Did not guarantee an **annual income** for all Americans
  - Did not create **jobs** for the unemployed
  - Did not promote **unionization**
  - Did not prevent businesses from shifting to **low-wage regions or overseas**
  - Did not address **deindustrialization** destroying manufacturing jobs

### War on Poverty Programs:
- **Office of Economic Opportunity (OEO)**: Oversaw all poverty programs
- **Head Start**: Early childhood education for poor children
- **Job training programs**
- **Legal services** for the poor
- **Scholarships** for poor college students
- **VISTA**: Domestic version of the Peace Corps for **inner cities**
- **Food stamps**: One of the most popular and successful — provided **direct aid** to the poor
- Philosophy: Required **poor people** to play a leading role in designing and implementing local programs → caused conflict with local political leaders used to controlling federal money; contributed to **grassroots radical activism**

### LBJ's Philosophy — Freedom and Equality:
- Resurrected FDR's phrase **"freedom from want"** — all but forgotten in the 1950s
- Echoing FDR: *"The man who is hungry, who cannot find work... that man is not fully free"*
- Sought to redefine freedom: Equal opportunity wasn't enough — *"You do not wipe away the scars of centuries by saying: Now you are free to go where you want"*
- Called for **"equality as a fact and as a result"** — not just equal opportunity but equal *outcomes*; this is an **early articulation of affirmative action philosophy**

### Results of the Great Society:
- Poverty rate fell from **22% to 13%** of American families during the 1960s (has fluctuated around 13% ever since)
- Sum spent was **too low** to end poverty entirely or transform poor urban neighborhoods
- By **1967**, the escalating Vietnam War caused the **War on Poverty to grind to a halt** as resources were diverted
- **Legacy**: The historic gap between whites and Blacks in education, income, and employment access has narrowed — but median white household wealth remains **ten times greater** than Black household wealth; nearly **a quarter of all Black children** still live in poverty

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The War on Poverty treated poverty as a **personal/cultural problem** (lack of skills/motivation) rather than a **structural economic problem** (lack of jobs, low wages). This philosophical distinction is testable. Also know *The Other America* by Harrington was the intellectual catalyst.

## XIV. THE CHANGING BLACK MOVEMENT

### From Access to Economics:
- Even at the movement's triumph, a crisis emerged: moving from **legal equality** (access to schools, accommodations, voting) to **economic equality** was far harder
- Black unemployment = **twice** that of whites; average Black family income = **little more than half** the white average
- Many whites believed the 1964–1965 legislation had **"fulfilled the nation's obligation"** → any further demands sparked charges of **"reverse discrimination"**

### Urban Uprisings:
- **1964**: First riots erupted in **Harlem** — battles between angry Black residents and the predominantly white police (seen by many residents as **"an occupying army"**)
- **Watts Uprising (1965)**: Black ghetto of **Los Angeles**; erupted **only days after** Johnson signed the Voting Rights Act
  - ~**50,000 participants**
  - Required **15,000 police and National Guardsmen** to restore order
  - **35 dead**, 900 injured, **$30 million** in property destroyed
- **Summer 1967**: Violence so widespread some feared **racial civil war**
  - **Newark**: **23 dead**
  - **Detroit**: **43 dead**; entire blocks burned; property damage in the **hundreds of millions**

### The Kerner Commission Report (1968):
- Johnson appointed **Illinois Governor Otto Kerner** to study urban rioting
- **Kerner Report (1968)**:
  - Blamed violence on **"segregation and poverty"**
  - Powerful indictment of **"white racism"**
  - Famous warning: *"Our nation is moving toward two societies, one black, one white — separate and unequal"*
  - Offered **no clear proposals for change**

### King's Chicago Campaign (1966):
- King launched the **Chicago Freedom Movement** — very different demands from the South:
  - End discrimination by **employers and unions**
  - Equal access to **mortgages**
  - Integration of **public housing**
  - Construction of **low-income housing** scattered throughout the region
- Confronted: Mayor **Richard J. Daley's** powerful machine + **ferocious white homeowner opposition**
- **Result: Failed** — King's tactics (marches, sit-ins, mass arrests) were **ineffective** against the North's less visible but still powerful racial inequality
- King's language grew increasingly radical: Called for a **"revolution in values"** and **"better distribution of wealth"**
- Called for a **"Bill of Rights for the Disadvantaged"** — an early articulation of **affirmative action** (*"after doing something special against the Negro for hundreds of years, the United States had an obligation to do something special for him now"*)

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** King's failure in Chicago showed that the movement's Southern tactics didn't work in the North, where segregation was *de facto* rather than *de jure*. This is a key point about the limits of the civil rights approach.

## XV. MALCOLM X

### Background:
- Born **Malcolm Little**; convicted of crimes as a youth; **converted in jail** to the **Nation of Islam (Black Muslims)**
- Nation of Islam preached **white evil** and **Black self-discipline** — a message of racial separatism
- Dropped his "slave surname" in favor of **"X"** — symbolizing Blacks' **separation from their African ancestry** (which slavery had erased)
- Became a leading spokesperson for the Muslims and a fierce critic of:
  - **Integration** — believed Blacks should control their own communities
  - **Nonviolence** — rejected King's approach
  - **Appealing to American values** — *"I don't see any American dream. I see an American nightmare"*

### Evolution:
- **1964 trip to Mecca** (Islam's spiritual home in Saudi Arabia): Witnessed **harmony among Muslims of all races** → began speaking of the **possibility of interracial cooperation** for radical change in the U.S.
- Founded his own **Organization of Afro-American Unity**
- **February 1965**: Assassinated by members of the **Nation of Islam** after his break with them

### Legacy:
- Left **no consistent ideology or coherent movement** at his death
- Most whites saw him as an **apostle of racial violence**
- His call for **Black self-reliance** resonated deeply with the **urban poor** and younger activists
- ***The Autobiography of Malcolm X* (1966)**: Became a massive bestseller
- Today: Streets, parks, and schools named after him — recognized as a complex, evolving figure
- Was the **intellectual father of Black Power**

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** Malcolm X's evolution is key — he **changed** his views after Mecca, moving away from strict racial separatism toward interracial cooperation. His assassination came from the Nation of Islam, not white racists.

## XVI. BLACK POWER

### Origins:
- **1966**: SNCC leader **Stokely Carmichael** used the slogan **"Black Power"** during a civil rights march in Mississippi — brought it to national attention
- Reflected anger over:
  - Federal government's **failure to stop violence** against civil rights workers
  - White attempts to **control movement strategy** (as at the 1964 Democratic Convention)
  - Civil rights legislation's **failure to address economic inequality** in Black ghettos

### What "Black Power" Meant:
- A **highly imprecise idea** — ranged from:
  - Electing more **Black officials** (moderate)
  - To the belief that Black Americans were a **colonized people** whose freedom required **revolutionary struggle for self-determination** (radical)
- Inspired **Black-operated community schools** combining traditional learning with **pride in African-American history and identity**
- **Cultural dimension** — "Black is Beautiful" slogan reflected:
  - Abandonment of "Negro" in favor of **"Afro-American"**
  - Popularity of African styles of dress
  - The **"natural" or "Afro" hairstyle**
  - Black beauty pageants
  - A **new sense of racial pride** and rejection of white cultural norms
- **SNCC and CORE** repudiated their previous **interracialism** (working with whites)

### Black Panther Party:
- Founded in **Oakland, California, 1966**
- Advocated **armed self-defense** in response to **police brutality**
- Demanded release of **Black prisoners** due to racism in the criminal justice system
- Members wore **military garb** — alarmed white Americans
- Also ran: **health clinics, schools, and children's breakfast programs**
- **Destroyed by**:
  - Internal disputes
  - A targeted campaign by **police and the FBI** — left several leaders **dead in shootouts**
- Former members who transitioned to electoral politics:
  - **Bobby Rush** — long-serving congressman from Chicago
  - **Charles Barron** — NYC city councilman
  - **Marion Barry** — mayor of Washington D.C.

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The Black Panthers are often tested for their **dual nature** — armed self-defense AND community programs (breakfast programs, health clinics). Also know they were founded in **Oakland** (not the South), reflecting the movement's shift to Northern urban issues. By **1967**, the Vietnam War escalation ended the War on Poverty — a direct connection between foreign and domestic policy.

## I. THE OLD LEFT vs. THE NEW LEFT

### What Was the "Old Left"?
- The **Old Left** = the radical/socialist tradition of the early-to-mid 20th century, dominated by the **Communist Party**
- Old Left characteristics:
  - Took the **Soviet Union as a model** society
  - Saw the **working class** as the primary agent of social change
  - Focused on **economic equality** and **social citizenship** (New Deal-style liberalism)

### What Made the New Left "New"?
- **Rejected** the intellectual and political categories of the Old Left
- Did **not** model itself on the Soviet Union
- Did **not** see the working class as the main vehicle for change
- Spoke a completely different language: **loneliness, isolation, alienation, powerlessness** in the face of bureaucratic institutions; a hunger for **authenticity** that affluence could not satisfy
- Focused on **participatory democracy** — individuals having direct say in decisions affecting their lives
- Greatest inspiration: the **black freedom movement** — more than any other event, the sit-ins catalyzed white student activism

### Why Did the New Left Emerge When It Did?
- **Baby boom generation** came of age in the 1960s
- Rapid growth in jobs requiring **post-high school skills** → explosion in college enrollment
- By **1968**: More than **7 million students** attended college — more than the number of **farmers or steelworkers** combined
- College students became a **major sector of the American population** for the first time

### What the New Left Actually Drew From (It Wasn't Entirely New):
- Its call for **citizen participation** → traced back to the **American Revolution**
- Its critique of the gap between American values and reality → echoed the **abolitionists**
- Its emphasis on **authenticity vs. conformity** → recalled the **bohemians** of pre-WWI era
- Its critique of **consumer culture** → drew from **1950s writers on mass society**

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The New Left's key concept is **"participatory democracy"** — direct citizen participation in decisions, not just voting. This is distinct from representative democracy. Know that it drew its biggest inspiration from the **Black sit-in movement**, not from traditional socialist politics.

## II. THE PORT HURON STATEMENT & SDS

### Books That Shattered the 1950s Consensus (1962–1963):
- **James Baldwin's *The Fire Next Time*** — angry voice of the Black revolution
- **Rachel Carson's *Silent Spring*** — exposed environmental costs of economic growth
- **Michael Harrington's *The Other America*** — revealed persistence of poverty amid plenty
- **Jane Jacobs's *The Death and Life of Great American Cities*** — criticized urban renewal, highways that destroyed neighborhoods, and the displacement of the poor; argued cities thrive on **density and diversity** — social interaction of people from different backgrounds on urban streets

### Students for a Democratic Society (SDS):
- An offshoot of the socialist **League for Industrial Democracy**
- **June 1962**: ~60 college students met at **Port Huron, Michigan** and adopted the **Port Huron Statement**

### The Port Huron Statement:
- **4/5 of the text**: Criticism of institutions — political parties, corporations, unions, the **military-industrial complex**
- **Key vision**: *"We seek the establishment of a democracy of individual participation, [in which] the individual shares in those social decisions determining the quality and direction of his life"*
- Defined freedom as **"participatory democracy"** — the standard by which students judged all institutions (workplaces, schools, government) and found them wanting
- By end of **1962**: SDS had grown to **8,000 members**

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The Port Huron Statement is the **founding document of the New Left** — know it came from SDS in 1962. It is compared/contrasted on the AP with the **Sharon Statement** (YAF, conservative, 1960). Both claimed youth as a cutting-edge force; both claimed to offer routes to greater freedom — but from opposite political directions.

## III. THE FREE SPEECH MOVEMENT AT BERKELEY (1964)

- **University of California, Berkeley** — a Cold War **"multiversity"**: immense, impersonal institution; enrollment in many classes approached **1,000 students**
- **Spark**: A new university rule **prohibiting political groups** from using a central campus area to spread their ideas
  - Even **conservatives** were outraged — barred from distributing their own literature
- Students created the **Free Speech Movement** in response
- **Mario Savio** — student leader; declared: *"Freedom of expression represents the very dignity of what a human being is.... That's what marks us off from the stones and the stars. You can speak freely"*
  - Compared the university to a **factory**: called on students to *"throw our body against the machines"*
- **Thousands** of Berkeley students participated in protests in the following months
- Program expanded from repealing the rule to critiquing the entire university structure — specifically, **education geared toward preparing graduates for corporate jobs**
- **Early 1965**: University gave in on the speech ban → activist declared students had reversed *"the world-wide drift from freedom"*

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The Free Speech Movement is the bridge between the civil rights movement and campus activism — it showed that **participatory democracy** was an issue even on elite college campuses, not just in the segregated South.

## IV. VIETNAM — BACKGROUND & AMERICAN INVOLVEMENT

### Why the U.S. Got Involved — The Cold War Framework:
- Few Americans understood Vietnam's **history or culture**
- Successive administrations viewed Asia through the lens of **Cold War geopolitics**
- Reduced a complex struggle for **national independence** (led by homegrown communists with widespread popular support) to a test of **"containment"**
- Key error: As one State Dept. specialist recalled about a 1963 policy meeting: *"The abysmal ignorance around the table of the particular facts of Vietnam.... They made absolutely no distinctions between countries with completely different historical experiences"*
- Walter Lippmann had warned at the outset of the Cold War about viewing the entire world through **"the either-or lens of an anticommunist crusade"** — Vietnam proved him right

### The Roots of U.S. Involvement:
- **Truman and Eisenhower**: Cast their lot with **French colonialism** in Indochina
- After the **French defeat**: U.S. financed the creation of a **pro-American South Vietnamese government** — in violation of the **Geneva Accords of 1954**, which had promised elections to **unify Vietnam** (elections were canceled because the U.S. feared Ho Chi Minh would win)
- By the 1960s: U.S. was committed to the survival of what it acknowledged was a **corrupt regime**
- Political fear: Presidents Kennedy and Johnson both feared being blamed for **"losing" Vietnam** the way Truman was blamed for **"losing" China**

### Kennedy & Vietnam:
- Kennedy's advisers saw Vietnam as a test of **"counterinsurgency"** — intervention to counter internal uprisings in noncommunist countries
- Despite increased **American aid and military advisers**, South Vietnamese leader **Ngo Dinh Diem** lost control of the countryside to the **communist-led Viet Cong**
- Diem resisted American advice to **broaden his government's base of support**
- Large **Buddhist demonstrations** against his regime → U.S. approved a **military coup** (October 1963) that led to **Diem's death**
- When Kennedy was assassinated the following month: **17,000 American military advisers** were in South Vietnam
- Notes from a White House meeting: Kennedy questioned *"the wisdom of involvement in Vietnam"* — but **took no action** to end American presence

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** Kennedy approved the coup that killed Diem — the U.S. is partly responsible for South Vietnam's political instability. Kennedy died with 17,000 advisers in Vietnam but no combat troops — the escalation to combat troops was LBJ's decision.

## V. LYNDON JOHNSON'S WAR

### The Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (August 1964):
- **North Vietnamese vessels** encountered an American ship on a **spy mission** off its coast
- North Vietnamese patrol boats **fired on the American vessel**
- Johnson proclaimed the U.S. was a victim of **"aggression"** and asked Congress for authorization
- **Gulf of Tonkin Resolution**: Authorized the president to take **"all necessary measures to repel armed attack"** in Vietnam
- Only **two members of Congress voted against** it:
  - **Senator Ernest Gruening** of Alaska
  - **Senator Wayne Morse** of Oregon
- The nearest the U.S. ever came to a **formal declaration of war** — but passed **without any discussion** of American goals or strategy
- During the **1964 campaign**, Johnson *insisted* he had **no intention of sending American troops** to Vietnam — then did exactly that immediately after his reelection

### Escalation:
- After reelection, the **National Security Council** recommended air strikes against North Vietnam and introduction of ground troops
- **February 1965**: Viet Cong attacked an American air base in South Vietnam → Johnson put the plan into effect
- **Dominican Republic (April 1965)**: Johnson also sent **22,000 American troops** to the Dominican Republic, fearing it would become **"another Cuba"** after a leftist military group tried to restore overthrown President **Juan Bosch** → outraged Latin Americans; but the operation's success *bolstered* Johnson's determination in Vietnam
- By **1968**: More than **500,000 American troops** in Vietnam

### The Brutal Conduct of the War:
- **North Vietnamese**: Mistreated American **prisoners of war** held at the **"Hanoi Hilton"** (sardonically named by the prisoners)
  - **John McCain** — POW who spent **6 years** there; courageously **refused early release** unless his companions were freed with him; later became **senator from Arizona** and **2008 Republican presidential nominee**
- **American air war**: Dropped **more tons of bombs** on the small countries of North and South Vietnam than **both sides used in all of World War II**
- **Deforestation chemicals**: Spread to destroy forests and deprive the Viet Cong of hiding places
- **Napalm**: Incendiary bombs filled with a **gelatinous form of gasoline** that clings to skin as it burns
- **"Search and destroy" missions**: Often did **not distinguish between combatants and civilians**
- **"Body counts"**: Weekly reports of enemy losses became an administration **fixation** — but the U.S. could not break its opponents' will to fight or make South Vietnam's government capable of surviving on its own

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The Gulf of Tonkin Resolution is one of the most tested items in this period — know that (1) it was based on a dubious/disputed attack, (2) only **two senators** voted against it (Gruening and Morse), (3) it was the legal basis for the entire Vietnam War without a formal declaration of war.

## VI. THE ANTIWAR MOVEMENT

### Why Opposition Grew:
- Casualties mounted; bombing continued; the **Cold War consensus unraveled**
- By 1968: The war had **sidetracked the Great Society**, torn apart families, universities, and the **Democratic Party**
- Young activists lost confidence in **"the system"** because the entire political leadership — liberal and conservative — supported the war
- **SNCC**: Condemned the war as inconsistent with a "free society" — asked how the U.S. could send Black men to fight for freedoms they didn't have at home
- **Draft inequity**: College students were **exempt from the draft** → the burden of fighting fell on **the working class and the poor**
- **April 1967**: Martin Luther King Jr. condemned Vietnam policy as an **unconscionable use of violence** that was draining resources from domestic needs — the most prominent American to speak out against the war at that point

### SDS and the Antiwar Movement:
- For SDS, the war was the **opposite of participatory democracy** — involvement came through **secret commitments and decisions by political elites** with no public debate
- **April 1965**: SDS invited opponents of the war to Washington D.C. — **25,000 showed up** (stunned organizers)
- **November 1965 rally**: SDS leader **Carl Ogelsby** linked Vietnam to a broader critique:
  - American interventions in **Guatemala and Iran**
  - U.S. support for **South African apartheid**
  - Johnson's **Dominican Republic** intervention
  - All rooted in **obsessive anticommunism**
  - *"Don't blame me for that! Blame those who mouthed my liberal values and broke my American heart"*
  - One reporter called the speech a **"declaration of independence"** for the New Left
- **1967**: Young men burning **draft cards** or fleeing to **Canada** to avoid the war
- **October 1967**: **100,000 antiwar protesters** assembled at the **Lincoln Memorial** → many marched to the **Pentagon**; famous photograph of protesters **placing flowers in rifle barrels** of soldiers guarding the building

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** King's opposition to Vietnam (1967) is a key moment — he was criticized for "mixing" civil rights and antiwar causes; many of his advisers told him to stay out of Vietnam. His position that the war drained the War on Poverty is a direct connection between foreign and domestic policy.

## VII. THE COUNTERCULTURE

### Origins:
- The New Left's original focus was **political** (participatory democracy)
- As the 1960s progressed, freedom expanded to include **cultural freedom**
- The war's destruction of young people's **belief in authority** was essential to the counterculture's rise
- By the late 1960s: millions of young people rejected the values of their elders — not just college students but also **young workers** (even though most unions **strongly opposed** antiwar demonstrations and countercultural displays)
- **First time in American history**: Flamboyant rejection of respectable norms in clothing, language, sexual behavior, and drug use became the basis of a **mass movement** (previously confined to artists and bohemians)
- Rallying cry: **"Liberation"**

### Key Contradiction — Consumer Culture:
- The counterculture in some ways represented **not rebellion but the fulfillment of the consumer marketplace**
- Extended the definition of freedom as **individual choice** into every realm of life
- Countercultural emblems — colorful clothing, rock music, images of sexual freedom, even symbols of Black revolution and Native American resistance — were **mass-marketed as fashions**
- **Bob Dylan's "The Times They Are A-Changin'"**: *"Your sons and daughters are beyond your command"*

### Drugs and Timothy Leary:
- **Timothy Leary** — Harvard scientist turned prophet of **mind expansion**
- Advocated **LSD** (psychedelic drug) as *"the freedom to expand your own consciousness"*
- **1967**: Organized the **Human Be-In** in San Francisco — urged a crowd of **20,000** to *"turn on, tune in, drop out"*
- Self-indulgence and **self-destructive behavior** were built into the counterculture

### Community & Events:
- Established quasi-independent **countercultural neighborhoods**: New York's **East Village** and San Francisco's **Haight-Ashbury** district
- Echo of **19th-century utopian communalism**: ~**2,000 communes** nationwide
- **Woodstock (1969)**: Rock festival in upstate New York; hundreds of thousands gathered to celebrate alternative lifestyle
  - Opening song by **Richie Havens** began with **eight repetitions of the single word "freedom"**

### The Deeper Meaning of the Counterculture:
- More than just sex, drugs, and rock and roll
- Personal liberation = **creative experimentation**, a life where **friendship and pleasure** eclipsed the single-minded pursuit of wealth
- Release from **bureaucratized education**, repressive personal behavior rules, and the **militarized state**
- **Underground newspapers**: Pioneered personal, politically committed journalism
- **Yippies** (Youth International Party): Introduced **humor and theatricality** into protest
  - **Abbie Hoffman** — yippie founder; showered **dollar bills** from the visitors' gallery of the **New York Stock Exchange**, bringing trading to a halt as brokers scrambled to retrieve the money

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The counterculture's contradiction is frequently tested — it claimed to reject consumer capitalism but was **quickly commodified and marketed**. Know that the counterculture was described as John Winthrop's (Puritan) nightmare — a massive redefinition of freedom as **rejection of all authority**.

## VIII. FAITH AND THE COUNTERCULTURE

### The Second Vatican Council (1962–1965):
- **Vatican II** — sweeping reforms in Roman Catholic practice: Mass now delivered in **local languages** instead of Latin
- Led many priests, nuns, and lay Catholics to become involved in **social justice movements**
- Produced a growing **split between liberals and conservatives** in the church

### Liberation Theology:
- A movement that swept **Latin America**: priests helped **mobilize rural peasants** to combat economic inequality
- Also reverberated among some **U.S. Catholics**

### New Religious Movements:
- **Jesus People ("Jesus Freaks")**: Saw the hippie lifestyle — long hair, unconventional attire, quest for universal love — as authentic expression of the **early Christian church**
- **Eastern religions**: Surge of interest in **Buddhism** (peace, rejection of materialism) and **Hinduism-derived practices** (yoga, meditation) — became popular with counterculture and even **suburban mainstream** for spiritual and physical well-being
- Some Americans traveled to **Tibet and India** to seek guidance from **"gurus"** (religious teachers)

### Religious Cults — The People's Temple:
- **Jim Jones**: Founded the **People's Temple**; combined intense spiritual commitment with strong criticism of **racism**; attracted a **racially mixed** following
- Led followers from Indianapolis → San Francisco → **Guyana**
- **1978**: Over **900 men, women, and children** perished in a **mass suicide/murder** ordered by Jones in Guyana

## IX. THE SEXUAL REVOLUTION & THE SECOND WAVE OF FEMINISM

### The Sexual Revolution:
- **1960**: Mass marketing of **birth-control pills** made possible the **separation of sex from procreation** — what "free lovers" had long demanded
- By late 1960s: **Sexual freedom** became as central to the youth rebellion as long hair and drugs
- **Rock music** celebrated free expression of sexuality
- Musical ***Hair*** — gave voice to the youth rebellion; flaunted **nudity on Broadway**

### The Status of Women When the 1960s Began:
- Only a handful of women held **political office**
- Newspapers divided job ads into **"male" and "female" sections** — female listings limited to **low-wage clerical positions**
- Major universities **limited the number of female students** they accepted
- In many states: **Husbands controlled their wives' earnings**
- **As late as 1970**: The Ohio Supreme Court held that a wife was *"at most a superior servant to her husband"* without *"legally recognized feelings or rights"*

### Betty Friedan & *The Feminine Mystique* (1963):
- Friedan had written pioneering articles in the 1940s on **pay discrimination** against women and **racism** in the workplace for a union newspaper (United Electrical Workers)
- *The Feminine Mystique* (1963) — its opening chapter, **"The Problem That Has No Name"** painted a devastating picture of talented, educated women trapped in a world that viewed **marriage and motherhood** as their primary goals
- Friedan called the suburban home a **"comfortable concentration camp"** — invoking the era's most powerful symbol of evil
- Richard Nixon had used the **suburban home** as an emblem of American freedom in Moscow in 1959 (**Kitchen Debate**) — Friedan's framing was a direct rebuke
- Deluged with letters from women: *"Freedom was a word I had always taken for granted. I now realized that I had voluntarily enslaved myself"*
- A few correspondents argued that making a comfortable home was *"what God intended"* — but the book focused national attention on the **gap between American rhetoric and women's reality**

### Legal Responses — Early Gains:
- **Equal Pay Act (1963)**: Barred sex discrimination among holders of **the same jobs**
- **Civil Rights Act of 1964**: Prohibited inequality based on **sex** as well as race (added by opponents as a poison pill; embraced by liberals)
- **Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC)**: Established by the 1964 Act; became a major force in breaking down **barriers to female employment** as it was deluged with complaints from working women
- **National Organization for Women (NOW) — founded 1966**: With Friedan as president; modeled on civil rights organizations; demanded equal opportunity in jobs, education, and political participation; attacked the **"false image of women"** spread by mass media

### Women's Liberation — The Radical Wing:
- A different feminist revolt emerged from **within** the civil rights and student movements — echoing the pattern of women abolitionists in the 19th century
- Women like **Ella Baker** and **Fannie Lou Hamer** played major roles in civil rights organizing — but many women in the movement were relegated to **typing, cooking, and cleaning** for male coworkers; some pressured into **sexual liaisons**
- **1965 SNCC memorandum** by female activists: *"There seem to be many parallels that can be drawn between the treatment of Negroes and the treatment of women in our society as a whole"*
- **SDS**: Women complained: *"The Movement is supposed to be for human liberation. How come the condition of women inside it is no better than outside?"*
- By **1967**: Women across the country forming **"consciousness-raising" groups** to discuss the sources of their discontent
- **Miss America beauty pageant protest (1968)**: Protesters filled a **"freedom trash can"** with objects of **"oppression"**: girdles, brassieres, high-heeled shoes, copies of *Playboy* and *Cosmopolitan*
  - They did **NOT** actually burn anything (the wooden boardwalk made it too dangerous) — but media invented the label **"bra burners"** anyway
  - Inside the hall: Banners reading **"Freedom for Women"** and **"Women's Liberation"**

### Core Ideas of Women's Liberation:
- Introduced the terms **"sexism"** and **"sexual politics"** and the phrase **"the personal is political"** into public debate
- Insisted sexual relations, conditions of marriage, and standards of beauty were **"political" questions** — not just private matters
- This idea **permanently changed Americans' definition of freedom**

### Abortion Rights:
- Radical feminists demanded repeal of **state laws banning abortions** — argued that without control over her own reproduction, *"woman's other freedoms are tantalizing mockeries that cannot be exercised"*
- **1969**: Feminists disrupted legislative hearings on New York's abortion law — the **14 scheduled "expert" witnesses** were **14 men and a Roman Catholic nun**

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The "bra burning" at the 1968 Miss America pageant is a classic MCQ trap — they **did not actually burn anything**. Also know that NOW (moderate) and women's liberation (radical) were two **separate strands** of second-wave feminism, just as the NAACP (legal) and SNCC (direct action) were separate civil rights strands.

## X. GAY LIBERATION

### Background:
- **Harry Hay**: Founded the **Mattachine Society** in **1951** — the **first gay rights organization** in the U.S.
- Hay wrote that gay Americans were *"the one group of disadvantaged people who didn't even think of themselves as a group"*
- Gay men and lesbians were **stigmatized** as sinful or mentally disordered; most states made **homosexual acts illegal**; police **regularly harassed** gay subcultures in major cities
- **McCarthyism** made persecution worse — viewed homosexuality as a **source of national weakness**
- Most gay people kept their sexual orientation secret — **"in the closet"**
- Mattachine Society's strategy: Persuade the public that gays were **average Americans** who happened to have a different sexual orientation

### The Stonewall Uprising (1969):
- **June 1969**: Police raided the **Stonewall Inn** in New York's **Greenwich Village** — a gathering place for gay people
- Rather than submitting to harassment as in the past, **gays fought back**
- **Five days of rioting** followed → birth of the **militant gay liberation movement**
- Gay men and lesbians stepped out of the **"closet"** to insist that sexual orientation is a matter of **rights, power, and identity**
- Within a few years, **"gay pride" marches** being held in numerous cities

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** **Stonewall (1969)** = the birth of the modern gay rights movement. Know it was a police *raid* that triggered *resistance* — not a planned protest. Know the location: Greenwich Village, NYC.

## XI. LATINO ACTIVISM

### Background:
- Long history of legal rights struggles among Mexican-Americans
- Mid-1960s: New **militancy** challenging economic second-class status
- Like Black Power: Emphasized pride in the **Mexican past** and the new **Chicano culture**
- Unlike Black Power and SDS: **Closely linked to labor struggles**

### César Chávez, Dolores Huerta & the UFW:
- **César Chávez** — son of migrant farmworkers; **disciple of King**; advocate of nonviolent protest
- **Dolores Huerta** — longtime labor activist; father was a migrant worker; mother owned a restaurant and hotel catering to low-wage farm laborers
- Together, **1965 onward**: Led **nonviolent protests** — marches, fasts, and a **national boycott of California grapes** — to pressure growers to sign labor contracts
- **United Farm Workers (UFW)**: As much a **civil rights movement** as an economic campaign; mobilized Latino communities throughout the Southwest; drew national attention to pitifully low wages and oppressive conditions for migrant laborers
- **1970**: Major growers agreed to **contracts with the UFW**

### Young Lords & Puerto Rican Activism:
- **Young Lords Organization** — based in **New York City**; modeled on the **Black Panthers**
- Staged street demonstrations protesting **high Puerto Rican unemployment** and lack of **city services** in Latino neighborhoods
- Famous protest: Dumped **garbage on city streets** to draw attention to the city's failure to collect refuse in poor areas

### Chicana Feminism:
- Like SNCC and SDS, the Latino movement generated **feminist dissent**
- Many Chicano and Puerto Rican men saw feminist demands as incompatible with the Latino heritage of **machismo** (an exaggerated sense of manliness including the right to dominate women)
- Young female activists countered that the sexual double standard was incompatible with **freedom for all members of *la raza*** (the race/people)

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** Dolores Huerta is frequently overlooked in favor of Chávez — know she was an equal co-founder of the UFW. Also know the grape boycott was **national** in scope, not just California. *La raza* = "the race/people" — Chicano identity term.

## XII. RED POWER — NATIVE AMERICAN ACTIVISM

### Background:
- **"Termination" policy**: Truman and Eisenhower administrations sought to **dismantle the reservation system** and integrate Indians into the mainstream — ending recognition of remaining elements of **Indian sovereignty**
- Many Indian leaders protested; the policy was **abandoned by Kennedy**
- Johnson's War on Poverty: Channeled increased federal funds to reservations
- Indian activists compared their status to **underdeveloped countries overseas** — demanded **self-determination**, not just economic aid

### Key Figure:
- **Clyde Warrior** — president of the **National Indian Youth Council**: *"We are not free in the most basic sense of the word. We are not allowed to make those basic human choices about our personal life and the destiny of our communities"*

### American Indian Movement (AIM):
- **Founded 1968**: Staged protests demanding greater **tribal self-government** and restoration of **economic resources guaranteed in treaties**
- **1969**: A group calling itself **"Indians of All Tribes"** occupied **Alcatraz Island** in San Francisco Bay — claimed it had been **illegally seized** from its original inhabitants
  - Occupation lasted into **1971** → launched the **Red Power movement**
- Results: Many Indian tribes won greater control over **education and economic development** on reservations; land claims suits won **monetary settlements** for past dispossession
- **Self-respect effect**: Number of Americans identifying as Indian **doubled between 1970 and 1990**

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** "Termination" is a specific policy name — know it meant ending federal recognition of Indian sovereignty. The Alcatraz occupation (1969–1971) launched Red Power the same year as Stonewall launched gay liberation — 1969 was a pivotal year for multiple movements.

## XIII. ENVIRONMENTALISM

### Rachel Carson & *Silent Spring* (1962):
- **Rachel Carson** — marine biologist
- ***Silent Spring* (1962)**: Exposed the effects of **DDT** — an insecticide widely used against mosquitoes, gypsy moths, and other insects
- In chilling detail: Showed how DDT **killed birds and animals** and **caused sickness in humans**
- **Chemical and pesticide industry response**: Launched a campaign to discredit her; some called the book a **communist plot**; *Time* magazine condemned Carson as **"hysterical" and "emotional"** — *words typically used by men to discredit women*
- **Result**: Launched the **modern environmental movement**; nearly every state quickly **banned DDT**

### The New Environmentalism:
- **Sierra Club** (founded 1890s to preserve forests): Membership **more than tripled** after *Silent Spring*
- **1969**: Television broadcast images of an **oil spill off California's coast** — killing birds and fish, despoiling beaches → exposed dangers of **oil transportation and ocean drilling**
- Unlike 1960s liberation movements (which emphasized personal freedom), environmentalism called for **limiting some freedoms** — especially the right to use private property however the owner desired — in the name of a **greater common good**
- Attracted the **broadest bipartisan support** of any of the new social movements
- Under **Republican President Nixon**: Congress passed the **Clean Air Act**, **Clean Water Act**, and **Endangered Species Act**
- **April 22, 1970 — First Earth Day**: ~**20 million people** (most under 30) participated in rallies, concerts, and teach-ins

### The Consumer Movement — Ralph Nader:
- **Ralph Nader** — lawyer; spearheaded the consumer movement
- ***Unsafe at Any Speed* (1965)**: Exposed how auto manufacturers produced **highly dangerous vehicles**; singled out **General Motors' Chevrolet Corvair** for its tendency to roll over
- **GM response**: Hired **private investigators** to discredit Nader
- When their campaign was **exposed**: GM paid Nader a **handsome settlement** → he used the money to fund investigations of **other dangerous products and misleading advertising**
- Laid groundwork for numerous **consumer protection laws and regulations** of the 1970s

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** *Silent Spring* is one of the most tested books of the era — know author (Rachel Carson), year (1962), target (DDT), and significance (launched the modern environmental movement). Also know that environmentalism **crossed party lines** — unlike other 1960s movements.

## XIV. THE RIGHTS REVOLUTION & THE WARREN COURT

### Overview:
- The rights revolution began in the streets but achieved **constitutional legitimacy** through the Supreme Court — historically the most conservative branch
- Chief Justice **Earl Warren** vastly expanded rights for all Americans, placing them **beyond the reach of legislative and local majorities**

### Civil Liberties & the Cold War:
- **June 17, 1957 — "Red Monday"** (named by conservatives): The Court moved to **rein in the anti-communist crusade**
  - Overturned convictions for: **advocating the overthrow of the government**, failing to answer questions before **HUAC**, refusing to disclose political beliefs to state officials
  - Warren: The government could prosecute **illegal actions**, but not *"unorthodoxy or dissent"*
- By Warren's retirement in **1969**: Court had reaffirmed **First Amendment protection** for even the most unpopular viewpoints and **dismantled the Cold War loyalty security system**

### Civil Rights Cases:
- **NAACP v. Alabama (1958)**: Struck down Southern laws requiring civil rights organizations to **make public their membership lists** — protecting organizations from state harassment
- **New York Times v. Sullivan (1964)**: Overturned a **libel judgment** by an Alabama jury against the *New York Times* for carrying an advertisement critical of how local officials treated civil rights demonstrators
  - The *"central meaning of the First Amendment"* = the right of citizens to **criticize their government**
  - Also declared the **Sedition Act of 1798 unconstitutional** — over 150 years after it had expired
  - **Created the modern constitutional law of freedom of the press**
- **Loving v. Virginia (1967)**: Declared unconstitutional the laws in **16 states** that **prohibited interracial marriage**
  - Case arose from **Richard and Mildred Loving** — married in Washington D.C. (since Virginia law barred them); returned to Virginia; **local sheriff entered their home at night** and arrested them; sentenced to **5 years in prison** (offered option of leaving Virginia instead); they moved to D.C. but later sued to return

### Criminal Procedure & the States:
- Court required states to respect the **Bill of Rights** protections, including:
  - Protection against **illegal search and seizure**
  - Right to a **speedy trial**
  - Prohibition against **cruel and unusual punishment**
  - Right of poor defendants to **publicly supplied counsel**
- **Miranda v. Arizona (1966)**: **5-4 ruling** — individuals in police custody must be informed:
  - Right to **remain silent**
  - Right to **confer with a lawyer** before answering questions
  - That any statements may be **used in court**
  - Created **"Miranda warnings"** — now standard police practice

### Democratic Fairness:
- **Baker v. Carr (1962)**: Established the **"one man, one vote"** principle — legislative districts must be **equal in population**
  - Overturned systems where **rural areas** had equal representation to much more populous **city districts**

### Church & State:
- **1961**: Unanimously struck down Maryland constitutional requirement that public officials declare belief **"in the existence of God"**
- **Engel v. Vitale (1962)**: **Prayers and Bible readings in public schools** violated the **First Amendment**
  - Kennedy noted Americans remained free to pray at home or in church
  - Most **unpopular Warren Court rulings**: **80% of Americans** favored allowing school prayer

### The Right to Privacy:
- **Griswold v. Connecticut (1965)**: Overturned a state law **prohibiting the use of contraceptives**
  - Justice **William O. Douglas**: *"The right to be let alone is the beginning of all freedom"*
  - No explicit Constitutional precedent — Douglas argued for a constitutionally protected **"zone of privacy"** that could be inferred from the **"penumbras" (shadows) of the Bill of Rights**
  - Initially linked privacy to the **sanctity of marriage**; soon extended to **individuals** (including unmarried adults and eventually minors) — an admission that law could not reverse the sexual revolution
- **Roe v. Wade (1973)**: Built directly on Griswold; created a **constitutional right to terminate a pregnancy**; declared abortion a **fundamental freedom**
  - Decided **4 years after Warren's retirement** but a product of the Warren Court era
  - Only **2 states** had banned contraception when *Griswold* was decided; *Roe* invalidated the laws of **46 states**
  - Provoked vigorous opposition that **continues to this day**

### The Legacy of the Rights Revolution:
- Griswold and Roe led to rulings and laws that accepted the **feminist view of the family** as a collection of **sovereign individuals** rather than a unit with a single (male) head
- Law enforcement began prosecuting **rape and assault by husbands against their wives** for the first time
- The rights revolution completed the transformation of American freedom from **entitlements for white men** into **an open-ended claim to equality, recognition, and self-determination** for all

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** Know all the major Warren Court cases and what each did. The most-tested: **Miranda** (police custody rights), **Griswold** (right to privacy/contraception), **Baker v. Carr** (one man, one vote), **Loving** (interracial marriage), **New York Times v. Sullivan** (press freedom/government criticism), **Engel v. Vitale** (school prayer). "Penumbras" is the legal concept behind Griswold's privacy right — the Constitution doesn't mention privacy but it can be *inferred*.

## XV. 1968 — A YEAR OF TURMOIL

### The Tet Offensive (January 1968):
- **Late January 1968**: Viet Cong and North Vietnamese troops launched **well-organized uprisings in cities throughout South Vietnam** — completely surprising American military leaders
- U.S. drove back the offensive and inflicted **heavy losses** — but the intensity shocked the public
- The administration had **repeatedly proclaimed victory was "just around the corner"** — Tet shattered that narrative on **national television**
- Leading members of press and political establishment began criticizing U.S. involvement

### LBJ's Political Collapse:
- **Senator Eugene McCarthy** (antiwar Democrat, Minnesota) announced he would seek the **Democratic nomination**
- Aided by student volunteers, McCarthy received **more than 40% of the vote** in the **New Hampshire primary** — stunning for a challenger against an incumbent
- Public support dissolved; Johnson **rejected** the military's request for **200,000 more troops**
- Johnson then stunned the nation: Announced he would **not seek reelection**
- **Peace talks** opened in **Paris**

### Martin Luther King Jr.'s Assassination (April 4, 1968):
- King was organizing a **Poor People's March** — planning to bring thousands to Washington to demand increased anti-poverty efforts
- Traveled to **Memphis** to support a strike of the city's **grossly underpaid Black garbage collectors**
- **Killed by a white assassin**
- Result: **Greatest outbreak of urban violence in U.S. history** — ghettos erupted across the country; Washington D.C. had to be **occupied by soldiers** before order was restored
- **Congressional response**: As a gesture to King's memory, Congress passed the **Open Housing Act** (last major civil rights law) — prohibited discrimination in the **sale and rental** of homes and apartments; had **weak enforcement mechanisms**

### Columbia University (April 1968):
- Students protesting Columbia's **involvement in defense research** and its plan to build a **gymnasium in a public park** (on land used by the Harlem community) occupied **7 campus buildings**
- New York police removed them in an assault leaving **hundreds injured** → led to a **student strike** that closed the campus

### Robert Kennedy's Assassination (June 1968):
- **Robert F. Kennedy** — seeking the Democratic nomination as an opponent of the war
- Assassinated by a **young Palestinian nationalist**

### The Democratic National Convention — Chicago (August 1968):
- Tens of thousands of antiwar activists descended on **Chicago**
- Delegates nominated **Vice President Hubert Humphrey** (pro-war) as the Democratic candidate
- Chicago police **assaulted marchers with nightsticks** — hundreds of injuries outside the convention hall; **pandemonium inside** it
- A later investigation called it a **"police riot"**
- The government then indicted **eight political radicals** for conspiring to incite the violence — the **"Chicago Eight"**:
  - **Tom Hayden** — SDS
  - **Abbie Hoffman** — yippie leader
  - **Bobby Seale** — Black Panthers
  - (plus five others)
- Five were found **guilty** after a tumultuous trial — but an appeals court overturned the convictions because Judge **Julius Hoffman** (no relation to Abbie) had been **flagrantly biased** against the defendants

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** 1968 is one of the most tested years in APUSH. Know: Tet (January), King assassination (April 4), RFK assassination (June), Chicago DNC (August). The Open Housing Act is often overlooked — it was the **last major civil rights law** of the era.

## XVI. 1968 AS A GLOBAL PHENOMENON

- Like **1848** and **1919**, 1968 was a year of **worldwide upheaval**
- **Television** carried events instantly across the globe
- Major international events of 1968:
  - **London, Rome, Paris, Munich, Tokyo**: Massive antiwar demonstrations; clashes with police
  - **Italy**: Students occupied university buildings, halting education
  - **France — May 1968**: Nationwide student uprising; millions of **French workers** joined, adding demands for higher wages and workplace democracy; resulted in a **general strike that nearly toppled the government**
  - **Czechoslovakia**: Reform leaders promised **"socialism with a human face"** → **Soviet invasion** ousted them
  - **Mexico City**: Soldiers fired on students demonstrating for democracy on the eve of the **Olympic Games opening** → more than **500 deaths**
  - **Northern Ireland**: Police attacked a peaceful march of **Catholics demanding an end to religious discrimination**, inspired by the American civil rights movement → marked the beginning of **"The Troubles"** — violent conflict that didn't end until the **turn of the 21st century**
- The **second wave of American feminism** found echoes worldwide
- In **France and Italy** (Catholic countries): Women's movements won significant legal changes — easier divorces, decriminalized abortion
- ***Our Bodies, Ourselves* (1973)**: Book by Boston women dealing frankly with women's health, pregnancy, birth control, sexually transmitted diseases → quickly translated into **20 languages**

## XVII. THE ELECTION OF 1968 & NIXON'S COMEBACK

### The Political Landscape:
- 1968's turmoil produced a demand for **public order**
- **Black militancy** produced white **"backlash"** — an increasingly powerful political force
- The unelected Supreme Court **"inventing rights"** fed the argument that **faraway bureaucrats** were overriding local traditions

### The Candidates:
- **Richard Nixon** — remarkable political **comeback** after losing the 1960 presidential election and the 1962 California governorship
- Campaigned as champion of the **"silent majority"** — ordinary Americans who believed change had gone too far
- Called for renewed commitment to **"law and order"**
- **Hubert Humphrey** — couldn't overcome the deep divide in the Democratic Party over Vietnam

### The Results:
- **Nixon**: **43%** of the popular vote — razor-thin margin over Humphrey
- **George Wallace** — running as an **independent**; appealed to resentments against:
  - Black gains from civil rights
  - Great Society programs
  - The Warren Court
  - Received **13%** of the popular vote
- Nixon + Wallace combined = a **considerable number of former Democratic voters**
- Message: Just **4 years after Johnson's landslide** ushered in the Great Society, **liberalism was on the defensive**

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** Nixon's "silent majority" and George Wallace's 13% together tell the story of the **conservative backlash** that would define American politics for the next 50 years. Wallace's vote came largely from **white working-class former Democrats** — the beginning of the Democratic coalition's collapse.

## XVIII. THE LEGACY OF THE SIXTIES

- **Expanded rights** and new understandings of freedom
- Made possible entrance of **racial minorities** into the mainstream — while leaving **urban poverty** unsolved
- Set in motion the **transformation of women's status**
- Changed what Americans expected from government: **clean air and water, medical coverage in old age**
- Undermined **public confidence in national leaders**
- Changed relations between **young and old, men and women, white and non-white**, and every institution
- Just as the Civil War and New Deal established **frameworks for future political debates**, so too did the 1960s — Americans would "refight the battles of the 1960s" long after the decade ended
- As America became more conservative, the Sixties were **blamed for every imaginable social ill**: crime, drug abuse, decline of respect for authority
- Yet: *"During the 1960s, the United States became a more open, more tolerant — in a word, a freer — country"*

## I. ROOTS OF THE CONSERVATIVE RESURGENCE

### Why Conservatism Rose in the 1970s–80s:
- **Economic problems**: Stagflation, deindustrialization, declining real wages → heightened appeal of:
  - Lower taxes
  - Reduced government regulation
  - Cuts in social spending to spur business investment
- **Foreign policy fears**: Perceived decline of American power → calls for **Cold War renewal**
- **Civil rights and sexual revolutions**: Produced **resentments** that undermined the Democratic coalition
- **Rising urban crime**: Reinforced demands for **"law and order"** and attacks on courts seen as too lenient
- **Suburban growth**: The predominantly white suburbs became a **bastion of modern conservatism** — residents were **fleeing cities** and their problems

### How Conservatives Changed Their Language:
- Abandoned **overt opposition** to the Black struggle for racial justice
- The fiery rhetoric and direct confrontation of **Bull Connor** and **George Wallace** gave way to softer appeals:
  - **"Freedom of association"**
  - **"Local control"**
  - **Resistance to federal government power**
- This language of **individual freedom** resonated nationally — especially in the white suburbs
- Like the civil rights and labor movements before them, conservatives **organized at the grassroots**:
  - Ran candidates even when they had little chance of winning
  - Changed policies of **local institutions**: school boards, town councils, planning commissions

### Neoconservatives:
- **Neoconservatives** = a group of intellectuals who were *once liberals* but came to believe even well-intentioned government programs did more harm than good
- Charged that the 1960s had produced a **decline in moral standards and respect for authority**
- Key arguments:
  - **Welfare** not only failed to alleviate poverty — it **encouraged single motherhood** and **undermined the work ethic**
  - **High taxes** and **expensive regulations** drained resources from productive enterprises, **stifling economic growth**
- **Conservative "think tanks"** created in the 1970s to refine and spread these ideas:
  - **Heritage Foundation**
  - **American Enterprise Institute**

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** Neoconservatives are *former liberals* who turned right — this distinguishes them from traditional conservatives. Their critique of welfare as creating dependency is a core conservative argument that MCQs love to test.

## II. THE RELIGIOUS RIGHT

### The Third Great Awakening:
- **Religious fundamentalism** surged during the 1970s — expanded conservatism's popular base
- Some denominations tried to bring religion into harmony with secular/material concerns; others **reasserted traditional religious values** — the latter approach attracted growing numbers
- Membership in **mainstream denominations** (Episcopalianism, Presbyterianism) **declined**
- **Evangelical Protestantism flourished** — some observers spoke of a **"Third Great Awakening"** (echoing the religious revivals of the 1740s and early 19th century)
- **Jimmy Carter** — first **"born-again" Christian** elected president — highlighted evangelicalism's growing influence; but unlike most evangelical political activists, Carter was **not** a conservative

### Why Evangelicals Turned to Politics:
- During the 1960s, many evangelicals in the **suburbs, South, and West** became increasingly alienated from a culture that seemed to **trivialize religion and promote immorality**
- Evangelicals of different denominations felt they had **more in common with each other** than with liberal members of their own churches
- Core political demands:
  - Reversal of Supreme Court decisions **banning prayer in public schools**
  - Reversal of decisions **protecting pornography** as free speech
  - Reversal of **Roe v. Wade** (abortion legalization)
- Religious Right proved adept at using **modern technology**: mass mailings, **televised religious programming** — to raise funds and spread their message

### Jerry Falwell & the Moral Majority:
- **Jerry Falwell** — Virginia minister; created the **Moral Majority** in **1979**
- Self-described mission: Wage a **"war against sin"** and elect **"pro-life, pro-family, pro-America"** candidates
- Falwell identified supporters of **abortion rights, easy divorce**, and **"military unpreparedness"** as **forces of Satan**
- Christian conservatives most agitated by the **sexual revolution** — saw it as undermining the **traditional family** and promoting immorality
- Believed American freedom had become **"out of control"** as a result of the 1960s

### Anti-Gay Mobilization:
- The growing assertiveness of the **gay rights movement** spurred fierce backlash
- **1977**: Popular singer **Anita Bryant** (familiar from televised orange juice commercials) led a campaign in **Dade County, Florida** → county passed an **anti-gay ordinance** under the banner **"Save Our Children"**

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The Moral Majority (1979) is one of the most tested organizations of the conservative movement — know founder (Jerry Falwell), year (1979), and that it represented the **Religious Right's** entry into electoral politics. Also know that "Third Great Awakening" is the term used for this evangelical revival.

## III. THE BATTLE OVER THE EQUAL RIGHTS AMENDMENT (ERA)

### Background:
- ERA originally proposed in the **1920s** by **Alice Paul** and the **Woman's Party**
- **Revived by second-wave feminists** in the context of the rights revolution
- Text: *"Equality of rights under the law"* could not be abridged **"on account of sex"**
- **1972**: Congress approved the ERA with **broad bipartisan support** and sent it to states for ratification
- Seemed uncontroversial — but aroused **unexpected protest**

### The Debate — Two Visions of Women's Freedom:
- **Supporters**: ERA offered a guarantee of women's freedom in the **public sphere**
- **Opponents**: Freedom for women resided in the **divinely appointed roles of wife and mother**
- The ERA debate reflected a **division among women** as much as a battle of the sexes

### Phyllis Schlafly — The Leading Opponent:
- Helped organize opposition to the ERA
- Argued the **"free enterprise system"** was the *"real liberator of women"* — labor-saving **home appliances** offered more genuine freedom than *"whining about past injustices"* or seeking fulfillment outside the home
- Opponents claimed the ERA would let men **"off the hook"** — denying their responsibility to provide for wives and children
- **Polls** consistently showed a **majority of Americans (male and female) favored** the ERA
- But: Thanks to the **mobilization of conservative women**, the ERA **failed to achieve ratification** by the required **38 states**

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The ERA's failure despite majority support is a key irony — it was **grassroots conservative women** (led by Schlafly) who defeated it, not male opposition. Know that the ERA was originally an **Alice Paul/Woman's Party** proposal from the **1920s** — it predates second-wave feminism.

## IV. THE ABORTION CONTROVERSY

### Roe v. Wade Backlash:
- Movement to reverse the **1973 Roe v. Wade** decision began among **Roman Catholics** (whose church condemned abortion under any circumstances)
- Quickly enlisted **evangelical Protestants** and social conservatives more broadly
- Core anti-abortion position: **Life begins at conception**; abortion = **murder**
- No compromise possible between this and the feminist position that a woman's right to control her body includes **safe, legal abortion**

### The Language of Rights on Both Sides:
- Both sides showed how the **rights revolution had reshaped the language of politics**:
  - **Pro-choice**: Exalted **"the right to choose"** as the essence of freedom
  - **Anti-abortion**: Called themselves the **"right to life"** movement; claimed to represent the rights of the **"unborn child"**

### Key Developments:
- **1976**: Anti-abortion movement's **first victory** — Congress ended **federal funding for abortions for poor women** through **Medicaid** (over President Ford's veto)
- **By the 1990s**: Fringe activists **bombed medical clinics** and **murdered doctors** who performed abortions
- Long-term result: Even where abortion remained legal, the procedure became increasingly **difficult to obtain** as hospitals/doctors stopped providing it and states passed laws limiting facilities

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The **Hyde Amendment** (1976, ending Medicaid funding for abortions) is the anti-abortion movement's first major legislative victory — often tested. Also note the framing: both sides used **rights language** — this is a major AP theme about how the rights revolution changed all political discourse.

## V. THE TAX REVOLT & CONSERVATISM IN THE WEST

### The Tax Revolt:
- Economic anxieties created a growing constituency for **conservative economics**
- Unlike the Great Depression, the 1970s economic distress inspired a critique of **government** rather than of **business**
- New environmental regulations → calls for **less government intervention**
- **Stagflation** increased the appeal of the argument that **government regulation raised business costs and eliminated jobs**
- To conservatives, tax reductions served a **dual purpose**:
  1. Enhancing **business profits**
  2. Reducing government **resources** — making new social programs financially impossible

### Proposition 13 (California, 1978):
- California conservatives sponsored; California voters approved a **ban on further increases in property taxes**
- **Results**:
  - A **windfall** for businesses and homeowners
  - **Reduced funds** for schools, libraries, and other public services
  - Many voters accepted this trade-off for lower taxes
- Demonstrated that **taxation could be a powerful political issue**
- Many other states **followed California's lead**

### The Sagebrush Rebellion:
- Long tradition in the West of claiming a **"colonial relationship"** with the rest of the country — pointing to **federal ownership of large swaths of western land**
- **Sagebrush Rebellion** = named after a bill passed by the **Nevada legislature in 1979**; a conservative upsurge directed at the federal government
- Historical roots:
  - **1920s**: U.S. Forest Service announced plans to increase **grazing fees and mineral rights** in national forests → Nevada ranchers went to court; were **rebuffed by the Supreme Court**
  - **1970s**: New **environmental regulations** (Clean Air Act) alarmed western **coal operators** → won fresh recruits
  - Carter administration's environmental policies seen as **closing the public domain to exploitation**
- Demands: Western states should control **grazing rights, mining development**, and access to public lands for fishing/hunting
- **Result**: Few concrete accomplishments — but underscored the **rising tide of antigovernment sentiment**
- Western Sagebrush rebels eagerly supported **Ronald Reagan's presidential candidacy**

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** Proposition 13 (1978, California) launched the modern **anti-tax movement** — it's the most important single event in the conservative fiscal revolt before Reagan. The Sagebrush Rebellion is less frequently tested but represents the **Western strand** of anti-federal-government conservatism.

## VI. THE ELECTION OF 1980

### The Political Climate:
- By 1980: **Carter's approval rating = 21%** — lower than Nixon's at the time of his resignation
- A **conservative tide was rising throughout the Western world**:
  - **1979**: **Margaret Thatcher** became Prime Minister of Great Britain
  - Promised to restore economic competitiveness by:
    - Curtailing the **power of unions**
    - Reducing taxes
    - Selling **state-owned industries** to private owners
    - Cutting the **welfare state**

### Reagan's Campaign:
- Reagan's 1980 campaign brought together **many strands of 1970s conservatism**:
  - Sunbelt suburbanites + urban working-class ethnics
  - Anti-government crusaders + advocates of aggressive foreign policy
  - Libertarians (freedom from restraint) + Christian Right (restore traditional moral values)
- Key message: *"Let's make America great again. The era of self-doubt is over"*
- Pledged to end **stagflation** and restore America's **dominant world role**

### Reagan's Appeal to White Backlash:
- Kicked off his campaign in **Philadelphia, Mississippi** — the same town where **three civil rights workers were murdered in 1964** — with a speech emphasizing belief in **states' rights**
  - Many white southerners understood "states' rights" as code for **opposition to federal civil rights enforcement**
- Repeatedly condemned:
  - Welfare **"cheats"**
  - **School busing** (for desegregation)
  - **Affirmative action**
- Republican platform reversed the party's **long-standing support for the ERA** and condemned **moral permissiveness**
- Reagan was **not personally religious** and was the **first divorced man to run for president** — yet won the support of the **Religious Right**

### The Result:
- Reagan swept to victory — carried Democratic strongholds including **Illinois, Texas, and New York**
- **Carter received only 41%** of the popular vote — a humiliating defeat for a sitting president
- Carter's post-presidential legacy:
  - Worked for **Habitat for Humanity**
  - Negotiated a cease-fire between **Muslim and Serb forces in Bosnia**
  - Arranged a peaceful transfer of power in **Haiti**
  - Won the **Nobel Peace Prize (2002)**
  - His presidency: **Almost universally considered a failure**

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** Reagan's **Philadelphia, Mississippi** campaign launch is one of the most-tested symbolic moments of the 1980 election — it was seen as a deliberate signal to white Southern voters. Also know that Reagan defeated Carter's **"era of self-doubt"** framing — he offered optimism and confidence as the antidote to 1970s malaise.

## VII. RONALD REAGAN — BACKGROUND & CHARACTER

### Reagan's Unusual Path:
- Originally a **New Deal Democrat**
- Was head of the **Screen Actors Guild** — the **only union leader ever to reach the White House**
- Emerged in the 1950s as a spokesman for **General Electric Corporation** — preached the virtues of **unregulated capitalism**
- His **1964 speech for Barry Goldwater** brought him to national attention
- **1966**: California voters elected Reagan as **governor**
- **1976**: Challenged President Ford for the Republican nomination — came **close to winning**
- **1980**: Won the presidency

### Reagan's Leadership Style:
- Opponents often **underestimated** him
- *"Rose at the crack of noon"* — relied on his wife to arrange his official schedule
- Unlike most modern presidents: Content to **outline broad policy themes** and leave implementation to others
- Was an **excellent public speaker** — optimism and affability appealed broadly
- Made conservatism seem **progressive** rather than reactionary
- Frequently quoted **Thomas Paine**: *"We have it in our power to begin the world over again"*
- Overall: **Remarkably successful** at seizing control of the **terms of public debate**
- On taxes, government spending, national security, crime, welfare, and "traditional values" — **put Democrats on the defensive**
- Was a **pragmatist**: Recognized when to compromise to protect his diverse coalition

## VIII. REAGAN'S ECONOMIC POLICIES — "REAGANOMICS"

### The Philosophy:
- Like FDR and LBJ, Reagan spoke of **"economic freedom"** and proposed an **"economic Bill of Rights"**
- But in contrast to predecessors: Economic freedom for Reagan meant **curtailing the power of unions**, **dismantling regulations**, and **radically reducing taxes**
- *"The right to earn your own keep and keep what you earn"* was *"what it means to be free"*

### Tax Cuts:
- **1981**: Reagan persuaded Congress to reduce the **top tax rate from 70% to 50%**; indexed tax brackets to inflation
- **Tax Reform Act (1986)**: Reduced rate on the **wealthiest Americans to 28%**
- These measures marked a sharp retreat from **progressivity** — the principle that the wealthy should pay a **higher percentage** of their income in taxes than others; one of the key tools 20th-century societies used to address **unequal distribution of wealth**

### Supply-Side vs. "Trickle-Down" Economics:
- **"Supply-side economics"** (proponents' term) = Reagan's economic program
- **"Trickle-down economics"** (critics' term)
- Core mechanism:
  - **High interest rates** to curb **inflation**
  - **Lower tax rates** (especially for businesses and high-income Americans) to **stimulate private investment**
  - Assumption: Cutting taxes would inspire Americans at **all income levels** to work harder (they'd keep more of what they earned)
  - Everyone would benefit from **increased business profits** → growing economy → government receipts would rise despite lower tax rates
- Reagan also appointed **conservative heads of regulatory agencies** who cut back on:
  - **Environmental protection**
  - **Workplace safety rules**

### Results of Reaganomics — Both Good and Bad:
- **Initial impact**: The **recession of 1981–1982** — the most severe since the 1930s
- **Recovery**: Long period of economic expansion as companies downsized, shifted production overseas, adopted new technologies (satellite communications) → became more profitable
- **Inflation**: Fell from **13.5% at the start of 1981** to **3.5% in 1988** (partly due to expanded oil production driving down energy prices, not just Reagan's policies)
- **By end of Reagan's presidency (1989)**:
  - Real **GDP had risen by 25%**
  - **Unemployment down to 5.5%**
  - These were **significant accomplishments**

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** Know both terms — "supply-side" (what Reagan called it) and "trickle-down" (what critics called it). The key mechanism: cut taxes on the wealthy → they invest → economy grows → everyone benefits. Know the recession of **1981–82** came *before* the recovery — this is tested as a limitation of the policy.

## IX. REAGAN AND LABOR

### The PATCO Strike (1981):
- **August 1981**: **13,000 members of PATCO** (Professional Air Traffic Controllers Organization) went on strike **in violation of federal law** (federal employees are prohibited from striking)
- Reagan **fired all of them**
- Used the **military** to oversee air traffic until new controllers could be trained
- **Impact**: Inspired many **private employers** to launch **anti-union offensives**
- The hiring of **permanent replacement workers** (previously a rare occurrence) became **widespread**

### Decline of Manufacturing and Unions:
- Manufacturing employment (where union membership was concentrated) continued its **long-term decline**
- **Steel industry example**: By mid-1990s, employed only **170,000 workers** — down from **600,000 in 1973**
- When Reagan left office:
  - Both **service and retail sectors** employed more Americans than manufacturing
  - Only **11% of workers** in non-government jobs were **union members**

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The PATCO firing (1981) is one of the most important labor events of the 20th century — it effectively broke the back of the labor movement by signaling that employers could fire striking workers. Know the acronym (PATCO = air traffic controllers) and the year (1981).

## X. THE PROBLEM OF INEQUALITY — THE "SECOND GILDED AGE"

### Growing Inequality Under Reagan:
- Reagan's policies + rising stock prices + deindustrialization = **considerable rise in economic inequality**
- By the **mid-1990s**: The **richest 1%** of Americans owned **40% of the nation's wealth** — **twice their share from 20 years earlier**
- The wealthy spent income not on productive investments but on:
  - **Luxury goods**
  - **Real-estate speculation**
  - **Corporate buyouts** that led to plant closings

### Who Was Hurt:
- **Middle-class families** (especially those with a wife who didn't work outside the home): **Income stagnated**
- **Poorest one-fifth of the population**: Income **declined**
- **Homeless persons**: Became a visible fixture on city streets — due to:
  - Falling investment in **public housing**
  - Release of **mental patients from state hospitals**
  - Cuts in **welfare**
- **Black workers**: Particularly devastated
  - Traditionally **"last hired, first fired"**
  - Had only *just* gained footholds in **better-paying manufacturing jobs** through civil rights gains and affirmative action — then lost them when factories closed
  - **South Gate, CA example**: The giant **Firestone tire factory** shut down in **1980** — just a few years after Black and Latino workers made their first employment breakthroughs there
  - When national unemployment reached **8.9% (end of 1981)**, the Black unemployment rate exceeded **20%**
  - Few Black workers had the education to take advantage of growing **"knowledge-based" industries** (technology, information services)
  - Overall: During the 1980s, **Black males fell farther than any other group** in wages and jobs

### The "Second Gilded Age" — The Culture of Greed:
- The 1980s like the 1890s: Widely remembered as a decade of **misplaced values**
- **Buying out companies** generated more profit than **running them** — making deals, not making products, was the route to wealth
- **1988 Nabisco/R.J. Reynolds merger**: Produced close to **$1 billion in fees** for lawyers, advisers, and brokers
- **Ivan Boesky** — Wall Street financier; declared *"Greed is healthy"* — ended up in **prison for insider stock trading**
- **"Yuppie"** (young urban professional): Earned high income in finance; spent lavishly on designer clothing and luxury goods — became a household word

### The Savings and Loan (S&L) Crisis:
- **Deregulation of savings and loan associations** (banks traditionally confined to financing home mortgages) allowed them to invest in **unsound real-estate ventures and corporate mergers**
- Losses piled up → the **Federal Savings and Loan Insurance Corporation** (which insured depositors' accounts) faced bankruptcy
- After Reagan left office: The federal government **bailed out** the S&Ls at a cost to taxpayers estimated at **$250 billion**

### The Federal Deficit:
- Supply-siders promised: Lowering taxes would **enlarge government revenue** by stimulating economic activity
- Reality: Spurred by **large increases in military spending**, federal spending **far outstripped income** → **large budget deficits**
- During Reagan's presidency: The **national debt tripled to $2.7 trillion**
- Reagan: Took credit for economic expansion while **blaming congressional leaders** for the deficit
- Despite all this: Reagan remained **immensely popular**

### The 1984 Election:
- Reagan won a **triumphant reelection**
- Opponent: **Walter Mondale**
- Mondale's notable choice: **Geraldine Ferraro** of New York as running mate — the **first woman candidate on a major-party presidential ticket**
- Result: Mondale carried only **his home state of Minnesota and D.C.**

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The national debt **tripling to $2.7 trillion** under Reagan despite supply-side promises of deficit reduction is a key contradiction. Know **Ivan Boesky** as the symbol of 1980s Wall Street greed. Know **Geraldine Ferraro** (1984) as the first woman on a major-party presidential ticket.

## XI. REAGAN AND IMMIGRATION REFORM

### The Immigration Reform and Control Act of 1986:
- Addressed the large numbers of immigrants who had entered from **Mexico without authorization**
- These workers were drawn by **strong demand for low-wage labor** that far exceeded the legal quotas established under the **Hart-Celler Act (1965)**
- Reagan viewed immigration as crucial to **America's economic strength**
- Promised to *"continue America's tradition as a land that welcomes people"* even as the nation regained control of its borders
- **Key provisions**:
  - **Amnesty and a path to citizenship** for nearly **3 million undocumented immigrants** who had put down roots in the country
  - **Penalties for employers** who *knowingly* hired undocumented workers
- **Result**: Unauthorized immigration **continued** — the law did not stem the flow

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** Reagan's immigration reform is a niche but testable fact — it provided **amnesty** for **3 million** undocumented immigrants. This is ironic given the Republican Party's later hardline immigration stance. The amnesty provision is the most-tested element.

## XII. WHERE REAGAN DISAPPOINTED CONSERVATIVES

- Sharply reduced funding for Great Society **antipoverty programs**: food stamps, school lunches, federal low-income housing
- But left **intact** core elements of the welfare state that many conservatives wanted to repeal:
  - **Social Security**
  - **Medicare**
  - **Medicaid**
- Did little to advance the **Christian Right's social agenda**:
  - **Abortion** remained legal
  - Women continued entering the labor force in **unprecedented numbers**
  - Reagan appointed the **first female Supreme Court Justice**: **Sandra Day O'Connor**
- **Bowers v. Hardwick (1986)**: Supreme Court upheld the constitutionality of state laws **outlawing homosexual acts** — a rare victory for cultural conservatives (reversed in **2003**)
- Gave verbal support to **school prayer amendment** but did little to promote its passage
- Launched **"Just Say No" campaign** against drug use (led by **Nancy Reagan**) — failed to halt the spread of **crack cocaine** in urban areas
- Reagan's Justice Department:
  - Cut back on **civil rights enforcement**
  - Worked to curtail **affirmative action**
  - But: Supreme Court continued to **approve affirmative action plans** by private employers and governments

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** Reagan's failure to eliminate Social Security, Medicare, and Medicaid — and his appointment of Sandra Day O'Connor — are frequently tested as examples of the limits of the conservative revolution. The Religious Right was largely **disappointed** by Reagan's actual governing record despite his rhetoric.

## XIII. THE WARS ON CRIME AND DRUGS

### Nixon's Foundation:
- Nixon called for increased **spending on policing and law enforcement** and expansion of **prisons** in response to rising crime and urban riots
- **1973**: Nixon declared an **"all-out war on the global drug menace"** — with little result

### Reagan's Expansion:
- Reagan championed **"freedom from fear"** — a conservative strand of freedom emphasizing **law and order**
- **Comprehensive Crime Control Act (1984)**:
  - **Reinstated the federal death penalty**
  - **Abolished the federal parole system**
  - Instituted **harsh sentencing guidelines** for judges
- Reagan brought national spending on criminal justice to a record high of nearly **$3.5 billion**
- **Anti-Drug Abuse Act (1986)**: Led to a surge in arrests and a **rapidly expanding prison population**
- **Prison statistics**:
  - **1980**: **50,000 Americans** in prison for **nonviolent drug violations**
  - **1997**: **400,000 Americans** in prison for nonviolent drug violations — **most of them Black and Hispanic**, who were **disproportionately targeted** by police
- **Result**: The war on drugs **failed** to seriously disrupt global supply chains or domestic demand

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The explosion in drug-related incarceration — from **50,000 to 400,000** between 1980 and 1997 — with the burden falling disproportionately on **Black and Hispanic** Americans is a major theme. This is the foundation of what critics later called **mass incarceration**.

## XIV. REAGAN AND THE COLD WAR

### Renewed Confrontation:
- Resumed vigorous denunciation of the Soviet Union — called it an **"evil empire"**
- Sponsored the **largest military buildup in American history** — including new long-range bombers and missiles
- **Strategic Defense Initiative (SDI / "Star Wars") — 1983**:
  - Proposed a **space-based system** to intercept and destroy enemy missiles
  - **Not remotely feasible technologically**
  - Would violate the **Anti-Ballistic Missile Treaty of 1972** if deployed
  - Appealed to Reagan's desire to **reassert American worldwide power**
- Persuaded **NATO** (over much opposition) to introduce **short-range nuclear weapons into Europe** to counter Soviet forces
- Renewed arms race and Reagan's **"casual talk of winning a nuclear war"** caused widespread alarm at home and abroad
- Early 1980s: **Nuclear freeze movement** — calling for a halt to development of nuclear weapons — attracted **millions of supporters** in the U.S. and Europe
- **1983**: Half the American population watched ***The Day After*** — a TV program unflinchingly depicting the devastation of nuclear war

### Overcoming the "Vietnam Syndrome":
- **"Vietnam Syndrome"** = widespread public reluctance to commit American forces overseas
- **Grenada (1983)**: Reagan sent troops to the Caribbean island to oust a **pro-Cuban government** — a small, quick success
- **Lebanon (1982)**: Reagan dispatched marines as a **peacekeeping force** during a civil war between the Christian government (supported by Israel) and Muslim insurgents
  - A bomb exploded at their barracks: **241 Americans killed**
  - Reagan **quickly withdrew** the troops
  - Lesson Reagan drew: The public would support **minor operations** like Grenada but remained **unwilling to sustain heavy casualties**

### Reagan's Third World Strategy:
- Generally relied on **military aid** rather than American troops
- Abandoned Carter's emphasis on **human rights**
- Embraced **Jeane Kirkpatrick's** (neoconservative writer, later U.N. Ambassador) idea:
  - **"Totalitarian"** communist regimes → **oppose**
  - **"Authoritarian"** noncommunist regimes → **assist** (even if they abused their own citizens)
- **Third World alliances**: Chile, South Africa — anticommunist dictatorships
- Poured funds into combating insurgencies against governments of **El Salvador** and **Guatemala** — whose armies and **death squads** committed flagrant abuses against their own citizens
- **El Mozote Massacre (El Salvador, 1981)**: Army massacred **hundreds of civilians** → the **State Department denied the event had taken place** even though it was widely reported in the press

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** **Jeane Kirkpatrick's distinction** between "totalitarian" (communist, oppose) and "authoritarian" (anti-communist, support) regimes is a key conceptual framework tested on the AP. The **Lebanon barracks bombing** (241 dead, 1983) is frequently tested — Reagan withdrew rather than retaliating, showing the limits of his anti-Vietnam Syndrome policy.

## XV. THE IRAN-CONTRA AFFAIR

### Background:
- **Contras**: Anti-Sandinista rebels fighting the leftist **Sandinista government of Nicaragua** (which had ousted American-backed dictator **Anastasio Somoza** in 1979)
- **1984**: Congress **banned military aid to the Contras**

### The Scheme:
- **1985**: Reagan **secretly authorized** the sale of **arms to Iran** (then at war with neighbor Iraq) in order to secure the release of **American hostages** held by Islamic groups in the Middle East
- **CIA Director William Casey** and **Lt. Colonel Oliver North** of the National Security Council set up a system that **diverted proceeds** from the arms sales to **buy military supplies for the Contras** — in direct **defiance of the congressional ban**
- Scheme continued for **nearly two years**

### Exposure and Consequences:
- **1987**: A **Middle Eastern newspaper** leaked the story → Congress held **televised hearings**
- Revealed a pattern of **official duplicity and violation of the law** reminiscent of the **Nixon era**
- **11 members of the administration** convicted of **perjury** or **destroying documents**, or pleaded guilty
- Reagan **denied knowledge** of the illegal proceedings
- Impact: Undermined confidence that Reagan **controlled his own administration**

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** The Iran-Contra affair has three key elements: (1) secret arms sales to **Iran**, (2) proceeds diverted to the **Contras** in Nicaragua, (3) **Congress had explicitly banned** Contra aid (the Boland Amendment). **Oliver North** is the key figure who ran the operation. Know that Reagan's denial of knowledge was disputed.

## XVI. REAGAN AND GORBACHEV — THE END OF THE COLD WAR BEGINS

### Mikhail Gorbachev:
- Came to power in the **Soviet Union in 1985** — bent on reforming the repressive political system and reinvigorating the economy
- The Soviet Union had fallen further and further behind the U.S. in **consumer goods** and relied increasingly on **agricultural imports** to feed itself
- Inaugurated two landmark policies:
  - **Glasnost** = **"political openness"** — greater freedom of expression and transparency in government
  - **Perestroika** = **"economic reform"** — restructuring the Soviet economy
- Gorbachev realized significant change required **reducing the Soviet military budget** → needed to negotiate with Reagan

### Arms Control Agreements:
- A series of talks **1985–1987** yielded **more progress on arms control than in the entire postwar period to that point**
- Key agreement: **Elimination of intermediate- and short-range nuclear missiles in Europe**
- **1988**: Gorbachev began pulling **Soviet troops out of Afghanistan**
- Reagan, who had entered office as an ardent Cold Warrior, left with **hostilities between the superpowers much diminished**
- Reagan even **repudiated** his earlier "evil empire" comment — said it referred to *"another era"*

> 🎯 **MCQ Watch:** **Glasnost** = openness; **Perestroika** = restructuring/economic reform — these are among the most frequently tested vocabulary words in this period. Know that Reagan's relationship with Gorbachev surprised both his supporters and opponents — he was willing to negotiate despite his hardline rhetoric.

## XVII. REAGAN'S LEGACY

### Contradictions of the Reagan Revolution:
- Intended to **discourage government dependence** by rewarding honest work and business initiative
- Instead inspired a **speculative frenzy** that enriched architects of corporate takeovers and stock market investors while leaving behind:
  - **Plant closings**
  - **Job losses**
  - **Devastated communities**
- Nothing proved more threatening to **local traditions and family stability** than:
  - **Deindustrialization**
  - **Insecurity about employment**
  - **Relentless downward pressure on wages**
- Nothing did more to undermine a **sense of common national purpose** than the **widening gap between rich and poor**

### Political Legacy:
- Left presidency with reputation **somewhat tarnished** by Iran-Contra and enormous deficits
- Yet: **Few figures have so successfully changed the landscape and language of politics**
- Reagan's Vice President **George H.W. Bush** defeated **Michael Dukakis** (MA governor) in **1988** partly because Dukakis couldn't respond effectively to the charge that he was a **"liberal"** — now a term of **political abuse**
- **Conservative assumptions** about the virtues of the **free market** and evils of **"big government"** dominated the mass media and political debates
- Those receiving public assistance came to be seen not as citizens entitled to help with economic hardship but as **a drain on taxes**
- During the **1990s**, these conservative ideas were embraced **almost as fully by President Bill Clinton (a Democrat)** as by Reagan and the Republicans

## XVIII. THE ELECTION OF 1988

### The Campaign:
- Television advertisements and **media exposés** now dominated political campaigns — politics sinking to **"new lows"**
- **Gary Hart** (Democratic front-runner, Colorado senator): Withdrew after newspaper reported he had spent the night with a **woman other than his wife**
- Both parties ran **negative campaigns**
- Democrats ridiculed Republican VP nominee **Dan Quayle** (Indiana senator) for factual and linguistic mistakes

- Half of all marriages ended in **divorce**
- More than **40% of births** were to unmarried women
- Fewer than **half of all households** consisted of married couples
- Women's weekly earnings: **82% of men's** (up from 63% in 1980)
- **Casey v. Planned Parenthood (1992)**: Supreme Court reaffirmed the right to abortion; overturned spousal notification requirement

## IX. IMPEACHMENT AND THE ELECTION OF 2000

### Clinton's Impeachment:
- **1991**: Senate hearings on **Clarence Thomas's** Supreme Court nomination became embroiled in **sexual harassment** charges by law professor **Anita Hill**; Senate narrowly confirmed him; the hearings raised national awareness of sexual harassment
- **1998**: Clinton's affair with **Monica Lewinsky** (a White House intern) became public
- **Kenneth Starr** (special counsel) issued a report accusing Clinton of **lying under oath**
- **December 1998**: House voted to **impeach Clinton** for perjury and obstruction of justice — he became the **second president to be tried by the Senate**
- **Early 1999**: Senate voted — neither charge reached a simple majority, far less the **two-thirds required** to remove him
- Polls showed Americans were more appalled by **Starr and Congress's obsession** with Clinton's private life than by Clinton's behavior — demonstrated how profoundly **sexual moral attitudes had changed**

### The Election of 2000:
- **Democrats**: **Al Gore** (VP) and **Joe Lieberman** (first Jewish VP nominee on a major-party ticket)
- **Republicans**: **George W. Bush** (Texas governor, son of President Bush) and **Dick Cheney**
- Gore won the **popular vote by ~540,000** votes (half of 1%)
- Everything hinged on **Florida** — amid confusion and ballot irregularities, Bush claimed a margin of a **few hundred votes**
- **Bush v. Gore (December 12, 2000)**: Supreme Court ruled **5-4** to **halt the Florida recount** — allowing Bush to be certified as winner
  - Majority argued the **Equal Protection Clause of the 14th Amendment** required a single standard for counting ballots (which was impossible in Florida)
  - Notably added: This ruling applied **only to this single case** — widely seen as a suspicious limitation
- The election exposed major problems: the **Electoral College** giving victory to the popular vote loser; a **voting system** that couldn't reliably determine voters' choices; campaign costs exceeding **$1.5 billion** — reinforcing the role of money in politics

## X. THE ATTACKS OF SEPTEMBER 11, 2001

### What Happened:
- **September 11, 2001**: Hijackers seized **four jet airliners**
  - Two crashed into the **World Trade Center** in New York City → buildings collapsed
  - One hit the **Pentagon**
  - Fourth plane: Passengers who learned of the attacks via cellphones **overpowered the hijackers** → crashed in a field near **Pittsburgh**
- ~**3,000 people died**, including nearly **400 police and firefighters** who rushed to rescue at the Twin Towers

### Al Qaeda and Osama bin Laden:
- Bush administration blamed **Al Qaeda** — a terrorist organization headed by **Osama bin Laden**, a wealthy Saudi Islamic fundamentalist
- Bin Laden had joined the fight against the **Soviet occupation of Afghanistan** in the 1980s and received **CIA funding and support**
- After the **1991 Gulf War**: Bin Laden's anger turned against the U.S. — outraged by **American military bases in Saudi Arabia** and U.S. support for Israel
- Al Qaeda had attacked the U.S. before:
  - **1993**: Truck bomb at the **World Trade Center** — 6 killed
  - **1998**: Bombings at **U.S. embassies in Kenya and Tanzania** — 200+ killed (mostly African embassy workers)

## XI. THE WAR ON TERRORISM

### The Bush Doctrine:
- Bush declared a **"war on terrorism"** with a vaguely defined enemy and no predictable timetable
- **"Either you are with us, or you are with the terrorists"**
- Demanded Afghanistan's **Taliban** government surrender bin Laden → Taliban refused
- **October 7, 2001**: U.S. launched air strikes against Taliban strongholds — operation named **"Enduring Freedom"**
- By end of 2001: Taliban driven from power; a **U.S.-friendly government** installed
- U.S. forces remained in Afghanistan **at least into 2019** — the **longest war in American history**

### The "Axis of Evil":
- **January 2002 State of the Union**: Bush accused **Iraq, Iran, and North Korea** of harboring terrorists and developing **"weapons of mass destruction" (WMDs)**
- Called them an **"axis of evil"**
- No evidence connected any of them to **September 11**; Iraq and Iran had actually **fought each other** in the 1980s

### The Iraq War:
- A group of **neoconservative policymakers** — **Vice President Dick Cheney**, **Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld**, and **Deputy Defense Secretary Paul Wolfowitz** — had wanted to oust **Saddam Hussein** since the start of the Bush administration
- They argued Iraqis would **welcome Americans as liberators** and quickly establish democracy
- **Secretary of State Colin Powell** warned it would require **hundreds of thousands of troops** and broad allied support — he was **marginalized**
- Bush announced a goal of **"regime change"** in Iraq, claiming Hussein had **WMDs** and was seeking nuclear weapons
- **February 2003**: Secretary of State Powell delivered a speech at the **UN** outlining these claims — every major assertion **later turned out to be false**
- **10–15 million people** worldwide demonstrated against the war in February 2003
- **March 2003**: U.S. went to war with only **Great Britain** as a significant ally, without UN approval — **Operation Iraqi Freedom**
- **Within one month**: American troops occupied **Baghdad**; Hussein captured months later, tried by an Iraqi court, **executed in 2006**

### The Aftermath — "Mission Accomplished":
- Bush appeared on an aircraft carrier under a banner reading **"Mission Accomplished"** — but the war was far from over
- **Looting and chaos** followed the fall of the regime
- A fierce **insurgency** developed targeting American soldiers
- **Sectarian violence**: **Shiite and Sunni Muslims** fought each other — Sunnis (a minority who had dominated under Hussein) vs. Shiites (the majority now seeking power and revenge)
- Eventually revealed: **Hussein had no WMDs** — the entire rationale for the war was false
- By **early 2007**: A large majority of Americans considered the **Iraq invasion a mistake**

## XII. CIVIL LIBERTIES AND THE WAR ON TERROR

### USA PATRIOT Act (2001):
- Passed quickly after 9/11 — **300+ pages** that few members of Congress had read
- Gave law enforcement **unprecedented powers**: wiretapping, spying on citizens, opening letters, reading email, obtaining personal records from universities and libraries **without a suspect's knowledge**
- **5,000+ foreigners** with Middle Eastern connections rounded up; **1,200+ arrested**; many held for months without charges

### Guantánamo Bay:
- U.S. set up a **detention camp at Guantánamo Bay, Cuba** for terrorism suspects
- More than **700 persons** from many countries detained there
- **November 2001**: Executive order authorized **secret military tribunals** for noncitizens — without traditional constitutional protections
- Justice Department declared American citizens could be held **indefinitely without charge** if declared **"enemy combatants"**

### NSA Surveillance:
- Bush authorized the **NSA to eavesdrop on Americans' phone calls without a court warrant** — a clear violation of existing law

### Torture Controversy:
- Bush officials argued the U.S. need not be bound by the **Geneva Conventions** or the **International Convention against Torture** when fighting terrorism
- Defense Department approved **interrogation methods** most observers considered torture
- CIA set up **secret jails in foreign countries**; conducted **"rendition"** — kidnapping suspects and sending them to countries where torture is practiced (Egypt, Yemen, Syria, Eastern Europe)
- **Abu Ghraib prison**: Photos emerged of American soldiers **beating, shocking, and humiliating prisoners** — severely damaged America's international reputation
- **2014 Senate report**: Confirmed torture was **systematic**, not the work of "a few bad apples," and that **no useful information** had been obtained from tortured prisoners

### The Courts Push Back:
- **Hamdi v. Rumsfeld (2004)**: American citizen held without charge in a military jail had a right to a **judicial hearing**; Sandra Day O'Connor wrote: *"A state of war is not a blank check for the president when it comes to the rights of the nation's citizens"*
- **Hamdan v. Rumsfeld (2006)**: Court (5-3) rebuked the administration — **Geneva Conventions apply** to terrorism prisoners; president **cannot unilaterally** set up secret military tribunals
- **Boumediene v. Bush (2008)**: Court (5-4) affirmed that **Guantánamo detainees have the right to challenge their detention** in U.S. courts; Justice Kennedy: Security consists not just in military might but *"in fidelity to freedom's first principles"*
